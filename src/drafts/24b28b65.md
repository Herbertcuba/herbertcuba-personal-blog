The conversation in agent development has shifted. A year ago it was capability — what can the model do? Today the question is reliability — why does it keep failing in production?

Teams are shipping agents that pass every test and still break in the real world. The fix they reach for is almost always the same: a better model, a tighter prompt, another retrieval pass. Then they ship again. It fails again. The cycle repeats.

I think the cycle repeats because reliability isn't a model problem. It's a context problem. And the way out isn't to find the right prompt — it's to treat context as something you iterate on continuously, across every layer of how your system is built.

That's a different kind of work. Most teams aren't doing it.

---

## The surface people optimize

Agent Design is where most practitioners spend their time. The system prompt, the role definition, the behavioral guardrails. This is context engineering in its most familiar form — and it matters. But it's one of many surfaces. Optimizing this one and leaving the others to chance is like engineering the engine and ignoring the road.

I've seen teams spend weeks on prompt quality while shipping agents that had no idea what environment they were running in, no clear definition of what "done" meant, and no way to tell if they'd succeeded. The prompt was clean. Everything around it wasn't.

---

## Where context actually breaks

**The infrastructure layer.** Before a single agent runs, your team has made foundational stack decisions: which cloud, and which agentic harness. AWS or Azure shapes what services and models are natively available. The harness — LangGraph, Hermes, OpenClaw, or something bespoke — determines how context flows through your system. What memory abstractions exist, how tool calls are structured, how handoffs between agents work. A managed harness abstracts this, sometimes helpfully, sometimes hiding exactly where context breaks. A local setup gives you control but means you design every context boundary yourself. The choice compounds.

**Operational Tooling.** The tooling built around your agents matters as much as the agents themselves. Dashboards, doc tools, status views, handoff interfaces — most of it vibe-coded, most of it built fast. This layer exists to help humans collaborate with each other and with the agents: what's running, what's stuck, what needs a decision. The context engineering problem here is subtle but consistent — if the tooling doesn't surface the right signals clearly, humans make poor steering decisions. You can have excellent agents and terrible operational visibility, and the result looks like agent failure when it's actually a human context failure. The quality of how humans see the system shapes what they ask the system to do next.

**Security context.** Telling an agent "don't expose PII" is a constraint. Giving it a mental model of what PII looks like, why it matters, and what to do when it's uncertain — that's context. The first produces brittle compliance. The second produces judgment. The difference shows up in edge cases, and edge cases are where agents are deployed most often.

**Business context.** This is the why. Not what to build, but why it matters, what the outcome looks like, what success means to the business. Without it, agents optimize for task completion. They finish things that don't need finishing. They miss things that do. Business context is the frame that keeps execution pointed in the right direction — and in my experience, it's almost always underdocumented.

**Specs.** Taking business intent and converting it into something an agent can actually act on — precise, structured, scoped. This is where most of the craft lives. The spec is the primary input to execution, and vague specs produce vague outputs every time. I think spec engineering is where human judgment matters most in the entire loop. It's the one surface you can't automate away.

**Acceptance criteria.** When an agent completes work, it needs to know what "done" means — not just functionally, but relative to the business objective. Without encoded acceptance criteria, agents pass all the functional tests and still produce something that misses the point entirely. That failure mode is harder to catch than a crash. It ships.

**Agent Design.** The system prompt, the role definition, the behavioral guardrails — this is context engineering in its most familiar form, and it's where most practitioners spend their time. It matters. But it's one surface among many. I've seen teams spend weeks perfecting this layer while shipping agents that had no business context, no clear acceptance criteria, and no operational visibility around them. The prompt was clean. Everything around it wasn't. Agent design is the most visible surface, which is partly why it attracts so much attention — and why the other surfaces get ignored.

**Orchestration seams.** In multi-agent systems, context flows between agents — from planner to executor, from implementer to reviewer. What gets passed? In what format? With what metadata? Most information loss in agentic systems doesn't happen inside an agent. It happens in the handoff. Teams obsess over individual agent quality and forget to engineer the transition.

**Operational feedback.** Agents need to be able to tell whether they succeeded. Most can't. They produce output and wait to be evaluated externally. Without structured feedback signals, deterministic checks, and escalation paths, agents that fail do so silently. You find out downstream, not in the moment.

---

## The pattern

What connects all of this is the same thing: agents can only act on what they know. And what they know — or don't know — is shaped by decisions made long before the system prompt is written.

If you only engineer context in one place, you haven't really engineered it at all. You've patched one surface and left the others to chance.

The teams I've seen do this well aren't necessarily running better models. They're more deliberate about what their agents know at every stage — in the environments they run in, in the specs they receive, in the tools they call, in the handoffs between them.

Context engineering is an operating model decision. Not a prompt decision.

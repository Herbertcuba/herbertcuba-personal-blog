A paper on context engineering has been making the rounds. The core claim: the quality of what your agent produces isn't just a function of the model. It's a function of what the model knows, when it knows it, and in what format.

I think that's right. But I think most teams are solving for the wrong surface.

When someone says "context engineering," they usually mean the system prompt. Maybe the RAG pipeline if they're thoughtful. They tune retrieval, clean up instructions, run a few tests. Then they ship — and wonder why the agent fails unpredictably in production.

My take: context engineering isn't a technique. It's a discipline. And it runs through every layer of how you organize work with agents.

I've been building a framework for this — APEX — which maps the operating model of agentic teams across three domains and nine subdomains. What I've realized is that context engineering isn't confined to one part of that model. It's the connective tissue across all of it.

---

## Three domains. Nine surfaces.

APEX organizes agentic work into Platform, Spec, and Config.

Platform is the foundation — the infrastructure decisions and tooling that make agents operable. Spec is the intelligence layer — how you encode business intent and translate it into work agents can execute. Config is the operational layer — how you design agents, wire them together, and verify what they produce.

Each domain has three subdomains. Each subdomain is a distinct surface where context engineering either works or fails.

---

## Platform: context for the environment

*Infrastructure* sits at the base, and it breaks into two layers. First, the platform stack: where does your compute live? Local deployments — a Mac Mini, a VPS — give you control, lower cost, and data locality. Cloud (AWS, Azure, GCP) gives you scale and managed model integrations. Most serious setups are a hybrid of both. Second, the agentic harness: what actually runs your agents? Open source options like OpenClaw, LangGraph, or Hermes give you full visibility and composability. Managed SaaS — Anthropic's agent infrastructure, for example — trades control for simplicity.

The context engineering implication cuts across both axes. Local infrastructure means you design your own context persistence. Managed harnesses abstract it — sometimes helpfully, sometimes hiding exactly where context breaks. The stack choice is never neutral. It defines what context surfaces you can even reach.

*Operational Tooling* is context engineering in its most compressed form. How you name a tool, what parameters you expose, what you write in the description — this is how an agent understands its own capabilities. Most teams write tool descriptions for humans. The agent reads them differently. A vague tool description doesn't just cause confusion; it introduces the wrong assumptions into every call that follows.

*Security & Compliance* — telling an agent "don't expose PII" is a constraint. Giving it a mental model of what PII looks like, why it matters, and what to do when it's uncertain — that's context. The first produces brittle compliance. The second produces judgment. The difference shows up in edge cases, and edge cases are where agents are deployed most often.

---

## Spec: context for the work

This is where context engineering lives most explicitly — and where most teams actually invest. But even here, the three subdomains operate on different levels.

*Business Context* is the why. Not what to build, but why it matters, what the outcome looks like, what success means to the business. Without it, agents optimize for task completion. They finish things that don't need finishing. They miss things that do. Business context is the frame that keeps execution pointed in the right direction — and it's almost always underdocumented.

*Spec Engineering* is the translation layer. Taking business intent and converting it into something an agent can act on — precise, structured, scoped. This is where most of the craft lives. The spec is the primary input to execution, and vague specs produce vague outputs consistently. In my experience, spec engineering is where human judgment matters most in the entire APEX loop. It's the one surface you can't automate away.

*QA Strategic* — acceptance criteria is context. When an agent completes work, it needs to know what "done" means — not just functionally, but relative to the business objective. Strategic QA encodes that standard. Without it, agents pass all the functional tests and still produce something that misses the point entirely. That failure mode is harder to catch than a crash. It ships.

---

## Config: context for the agents themselves

*Agent Design* is where most practitioners spend their time, and it matters. The system prompt, the role definition, the behavioral guardrails — this is context engineering in its most familiar form. But it's one of nine surfaces. Optimizing this one alone and leaving the others to chance is like engineering the engine and ignoring the road.

*Orchestration Design* is context engineering at the seam. In multi-agent systems, context flows between agents — from planner to executor, from implementer to reviewer. What gets passed? In what format? With what metadata? Most information loss in agentic systems doesn't happen inside an agent. It happens in the handoff. Teams obsess over individual agent quality and forget to engineer the transition.

*QA Operational* — agents need to be able to tell whether they succeeded. Most can't. They produce output and wait to be evaluated externally. Operational QA is the runtime context that closes the loop: structured feedback signals, deterministic checks, escalation paths. Without it, agents that fail do so silently. You find out downstream, not in the moment.

---

## The implication

If you only engineer context in one place, you haven't really engineered it at all. You've patched one surface and left eight others to chance.

The teams doing this well aren't necessarily running better models. They're more deliberate about what their agents know at every stage — in the environments they run in, in the specs they receive, in the tools they call, in the handoffs between them.

Context engineering is an operating model decision. Not a prompt decision.

APEX maps nine surfaces where context either holds or breaks. The point isn't to make the framework more complex. It's to make the failure modes visible — so when something goes wrong, you know which surface to look at, not just which agent to blame.

AI token prices dropped 280x in 24 months. They're still falling at roughly 10x per year. That's not a pricing trend — that's cognition becoming a commodity faster than any input cost in economic history.

Peter Diamandis published a piece this week building on Elon Musk's projection that global GDP could hit $1.2 quadrillion within a decade — roughly a 10x expansion from today. The math he runs is interesting: if most of that value creation concentrates among foundational builders, and only a relatively small number of people do the genuinely generative work, then each of those people needs to produce something in the neighborhood of $10 billion in personal value to carry their share. He frames it as a motivational target. A quota to aim for.

I find the calculation compelling and the framing slightly off. Not wrong — off. The $10B number is worth thinking about. But the mental model underneath it is still operating in a world where human output is the relevant unit of production.

That world is ending fast. Dario Amodei puts AGI arrival somewhere in late 2026 or 2027 — "a country of geniuses in a datacenter" is his phrase for it. Musk puts ASI by around 2031, digital intelligence that would exceed the sum of all human intelligence combined. Humanoid labor is tracking from $30/day toward under $1/hour as the hardware scales. AlphaFold has already mapped 200 million protein structures. The cognitive heavy lifting in science — the part we assumed required human researchers for decades — is largely done by machines now.

So when Diamandis says you need a $10B personal quota, my question is: denominated in what? Produced how? Because if intelligence is near-free and physical labor trends toward zero cost, then the question isn't "how do I produce $10B of value?" The question is structural: what does the economy actually reward from humans when most production is no longer human?

That's a different question. A harder one.

I think the answer has something to do with the difference between output and judgment. AI is extraordinarily good at output. Writing, analysis, code, synthesis, pattern recognition across large datasets — it does all of that faster and cheaper than people, and the gap is widening. If your contribution to an economy is primarily throughput, the math is brutal. The machines win on volume at near-zero marginal cost.

But there are things that are harder to proxy. Direction is one. Knowing what problem to work on before you can fully specify it, before the outcome is measurable — that requires a kind of judgment that depends on context accumulated over years, on caring about outcomes in a way that has personal stakes attached. An AI can optimize a given objective function well. Choosing which objective function matters right now, given everything you know about this particular market and these particular people and this particular moment — that's harder to delegate.

Taste is another. I don't mean aesthetic preference. I mean the ability to look at something and feel, before you can fully articulate why, whether it's right or whether it's drifting. That signal is fast. It comes from a lot of compressed experience. And it degrades quickly when it isn't connected to skin in the game.

Trust relationships are a third. Not networking in the vague career advice sense. The specific kind of trust that forms when two people have worked through something difficult together and know how each other behaves under pressure. That's not replicable. It accretes slowly. And in a world where AI can execute almost anything, the decision about who executes what — and why you believe they'll do it the way you'd want it done — matters more than it did when execution itself was the constraint.

The $10B frame is worth keeping. It correctly captures that the coming economic expansion is massive, that the distribution won't be even, and that the people who produce real foundational value will be operating at a different scale than before. But the model needs updating in one specific way: the quota isn't about volume of output. Output is cheap now. The quota is about quality of judgment at the moments that matter — the calls that require you to be in the room, accountable, with full context, where the cost of being wrong falls on you.

My take is that the Organizational Singularity Diamandis describes — rebuild every org chart assuming cognition and labor both trend to zero cost — points in exactly this direction. What you're left with after you remove the tasks machines can do is a set of roles that are fundamentally about decision architecture. Who sets the direction. Who holds the accountability. Who has the context to know when the model is wrong even when the model is confident.

That's the quota. Not $10B in output. $10B in judgment, well-placed, at moments that don't forgive error.

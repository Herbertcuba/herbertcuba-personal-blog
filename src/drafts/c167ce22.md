McKinsey just published their *State of Marketing Europe 2026* — 500 European CMOs, their priorities, their pain points, their bets. It's a rigorous, well-structured report. I read it twice.

The ranking that stopped me: **Branding sits at #1** on the list of most important topics. **Gen AI sits at #17 out of 20.**

Most people will read that as a reassuring signal. "See? Brand still matters. AI was overhyped." They'll share the chart, feel validated, and go back to whatever they were doing.

That reading misses everything important.

---

## The signal inside the ranking

The CMOs aren't wrong to prioritize brand. But the reason they're right has nothing to do with the reason most of them *think* they're right.

Here's the data they buried a few pages later: the 6% of CMOs who are mature in generative AI are reporting **22% efficiency gains**. The 89% who haven't invested? Zero. Not incremental. Zero.

So we have two parallel truths running at the same time. Emotional brand-building is rising as the primary competitive battleground. And AI capability is becoming a hard filter — one that separates companies operating at a different cost structure entirely from everyone else.

Most people frame this as a tension. Brand *or* AI. Human *or* machine. I think that framing is the problem.

---

## What the **Digital Singularity** actually predicts

In my book *The Digital Singularity Shift*, I argued that AI is the **Fifth Acceleration** — after speech, writing, printing, and the internet. Each prior acceleration collapsed a category of friction. AI collapses *functional excellence* itself.

The old utility equation looked like this:

**U = (f₁ + f₂ + f₃ + …) + N**

Product features, speed, reliability, efficiency — stacked up, summed up, pitched against competitors. Brand narrative (N) was the bonus layer on top.

The new equation is:

**U = βN**

When AI drives functional performance toward commodity — when "better" becomes baseline, when 6% of your competitors can produce the same output as your entire operations team in a fraction of the time — the functional stack collapses to zero. What's left is *β* (the emotional weight the brand carries) multiplied by *N* (the story it tells).

The CMOs ranking branding at #1 are responding to something real. The market is telling them that functional differentiation is dying. They feel it even if they can't name it.

McKinsey describes the symptoms clearly. What they don't offer is the mechanism.

---

## The Bain pyramid, read correctly

Bain's Elements of Value pyramid has been around for years — functional value at the base, emotional above it, identity above that, purpose and social impact at the peak. Most strategists use it as a checklist. "Are we hitting all levels?"

My take: in the AI era, it's not a checklist. It's a **survival map**.

Functional benefits are increasingly what AI produces by default, at near-zero marginal cost. Your competitors can match your features not because they outspend you on R&D, but because the same AI stack is available to all of them. Functional differentiation doesn't die because someone copies it. It dies because it becomes free.

The higher you operate on that pyramid, the more immune you are to that compression. Emotional resonance isn't replicable by an algorithm. Identity alignment isn't scriptable. Belonging to a movement is, by definition, scarce.

McKinsey quotes this directly from their report: *"The role of brands is shifting from providing functional benefits to fostering emotional relevance and trust. While algorithms take over tasks like product comparison and efficiency, emotional attachment and authenticity will become the primary drivers of brand value."*

That's precisely the **Digital Singularity Shift** in their language.

They name the destination. The equation is: U = βN.

---

## "Authentic" isn't what most brands think it means

The third pillar of the McKinsey data is where things get interesting — and where most marketing departments will get it exactly backwards.

70% of CMOs say purpose-driven, authentic brand experiences are now essential. "Raw, unpolished" campaigns are rising. Anti-advertising is rising. User-generated content is rising.

The instinct most CMOs will have: *make things look less produced*. Shoot on iPhone. Add some grain. Post behind-the-scenes content. Hire a creator who speaks off the cuff.

That's not what's being asked for.

Consider the case studies McKinsey pulls: Bottega Veneta's "Craft is our Language" campaign. Doritos' Triangle Tracker — 4 million scans, 3 billion impressions, built on the absurdity of a chip shape as cultural touchpoint. Louis Vuitton's Pharrell Williams cultural shows — 775 million views, not from advertising spend but from genuine cultural stakes.

These aren't "unpolished." They're something more precise: **sincere about the mission, ironic about the presentation**.

I call this **Metamodernism** — the only cultural operating system that actually passes the Gen Z authenticity filter. 81% of Gen Z stop purchasing from brands that feel inauthentic (Adobe, 2023). But what Gen Z reads as inauthentic isn't rough edges or lo-fi aesthetics. It's the *gap between what you claim to be and what you actually do*. The absence of a real point of view. The brand that takes no risks because it's trying to offend no one.

The wink tells them you know they know it's marketing. The sincere mission tells them it matters anyway. Both together — that's the only thing that cuts through a world where the average person is exposed to 4,000 to 10,000 ads per day, and click-through rates have dropped 40%+ from fatigue.

McKinsey calls this "interactive branding." The phenomenon is the same. The vocabulary just differs.

## What the 89% number actually means

Let me return to that buried statistic. 89% of the CMOs surveyed report zero efficiency gains from generative AI.

The tempting read: AI isn't delivering yet. Give it time.

The correct read: a filter is already operating. It is silently separating companies into two categories — those operating at a new cost structure, and those who haven't even started. The gap compounds every quarter.

In *The Digital Singularity Shift*, I wrote: *"The digital singularity is not a threat. It's a filter. It will filter out everything that's mediocre, generic, and soulless — because AI can produce that infinitely and for free."*

The 6% with mature AI capability aren't ahead because they have better technology. They're ahead because they made a decision earlier. That decision is now a structural advantage, not a temporary one.

The companies that will disappear in the next five years won't disappear dramatically. They'll disappear the way the Kodak moment disappears — not with a bang, but with the quiet realization that the thing they were selling, someone else is now giving away.

---

## The archetype that holds both sides

There's an archetype I outline in the book — the **Reality Maker** — the operator who understands that branding and AI are not competing priorities. They are two faces of the same shift.

Brand without AI capability: beautiful, but operating at a cost structure that cannot compete. Authentic storytelling on top of inefficient operations — a margin problem waiting to happen.

AI without brand: efficient, but producing the kind of output the market is already drowning in. Speed and scale in the service of things nobody cares about.

The Reality Maker holds both simultaneously. They use AI to compress time and cost at the operational layer — freeing up human energy and capital for what AI cannot do: take a genuine cultural position, carry a real point of view, build something people actually believe in.

The McKinsey data points directly at this. They just don't have a name for it.

---

## The question I can't answer yet

Here's what I don't know: how many CMOs will read this data, feel confirmed in their brand-first instinct, and use it as a reason to delay serious AI investment. And how many will read it and understand that the 6% producing 22% efficiency gains are building a moat that will be very, very hard to cross two years from now.

The data supports both readings. The second one is the harder move — politically, operationally, culturally. It requires admitting that the organization needs to change in ways that aren't comfortable, not just communicate in ways that feel fresher.

What I'm genuinely curious about is whether the brand instinct and the AI capability build can happen at the same time inside real organizations, or whether the structures that make brand-building possible — long-term thinking, creative risk, cultural patience — are fundamentally at odds with the structures that make AI transformation happen.

Maybe the question is less about priority order and more about organizational design.

I don't have a clean answer. But I think that's the right question to be sitting with.

For decades, the rule was simple: data is your edge. Your customer records, your codebase, your Slack history, your support tickets. That corpus is the thing competitors can't copy. It's the competitive advantage.

So what happens when you hand all of that to a frontier model as context?

Nate B. Jones put it cleanly last week: you're effectively renting your own context back to yourself. Every Slack message that flows through Claude's context window, every document indexed into a provider's RAG pipeline, every fine-tune on proprietary code, that's you building a competitive edge around someone else's business. The model provider becomes the only entity that can reason over your own knowledge base. You still own the data. You just can't use it without them.

## The old edge ran in your direction

In the SaaS era, data was alpha. Your CRM, your analytics warehouse, your event stream. You poured data in, you extracted insight. The vendor built the tool, but the data stayed yours, and the leverage ran in your direction: more data meant better decisions, better product, tighter feedback loops. The vendor was replaceable. The data wasn't.

The agent era inverts the arrow. When you pipe your Slack into Claude's context, when you let a provider's RAG pipeline index your internal docs, when you fine-tune on your codebase, you're not just using a tool. You're transferring the ability to reason over your own knowledge to a third party. The data is still technically yours. The capacity to act on it now belongs to the model. And the model only runs on their infrastructure, under their terms, at their price.

## The Borg was polite about it

The Borg didn't conquer through violence. Assimilation was efficient. You handed over your distinctiveness. It got absorbed into the collective. And then you couldn't function without it, because the collective now held everything you knew, organized in ways you could access only through it.

That's close to what's happening when an agent has been reading your Slack for six months, indexing your docs, learning your codebase patterns. The model has absorbed your context. Not stored it the way a database does. Absorbed it the way a colleague does. And you can't un-absorb it any more than the Borg can un-assimilate a species.

## The window is closing

Here's what makes this urgent rather than hypothetical. GLM 5.2 is free and matches Claude on real work. Murati's Thinking Machines just released a 975B open-weights model called Inkling, and it hit the HN front page. The open-weights frontier is catching up fast.

That sounds like good news for portability, and it is, eventually. But right now, while the gap between proprietary and open still exists, every team I talk to is defaulting to "just use the Claude API." Every integration, every RAG pipeline, every agent scaffold gets built around Claude's context window, Claude's tool format, Claude's specific behaviors. By the time the open alternative is genuinely equivalent, you'll have months of accumulated context living inside a provider's infrastructure, formatted for their model, indexed in their pipeline.

My take: the portability problem isn't a future migration. It's an architectural decision you're making today, probably without realizing it. Every context window you feed, every RAG index you build, every fine-tune you run is a brick in a wall you'll have to dismantle later if you want to move.

## Make portability a constraint, not a plan

Data portability needs to be an architectural constraint right now, not something you'll "address later." Because later, the context will have compounded. The model will know your codebase better than your new hires do. The RAG pipeline will be the only way anyone finds anything in your docs. The fine-tune will encode patterns nobody documented. At that point, migrating isn't a technical exercise. It's organizational amnesia.

I think the teams that get this right are building provider-agnostic from day one. Their context layer is abstracted. Their RAG pipeline runs on infrastructure they control. Their agents can swap the underlying model without losing the accumulated context. This isn't paranoia. It's the same principle as avoiding cloud lock-in, except the stakes are higher, because what's locked in isn't just infrastructure. It's the ability to reason over your own knowledge.

The SaaS era taught us that the deepest lock-in is data dependency, not API contracts. The agent era teaches the same lesson with sharper edges. When the data you're depending on is your own, and the only way to access its value is through a provider's model, you don't own your edge anymore. You rent it back. And the landlord can change the terms whenever they want.

Build for portability now, or plan to negotiate later from a position with no leverage.

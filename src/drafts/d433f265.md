At WWDC 2026, Apple spent a lot of time on stage talking about Siri. Smarter Siri. More capable Siri. Siri that finally understands context. Most people walked away thinking Apple had rejoined the AI race. I walked away thinking about something else entirely.

App Intents.

That's the real story. Apple has been quietly building an architecture that lets the operating system reach *into* your app and call specific capabilities on behalf of an agent. You register your app's actions — create a note, send a payment, start a workout, book a table — and those actions become callable by the OS. Siri doesn't need to open your app and navigate through a UI. It invokes the intent directly. The app becomes a set of functions that an intelligent layer can orchestrate.

I think most coverage missed this because it sounds like a developer-facing API story. Dry. Technical. But it's actually a strategic inflection point disguised as a platform update.

Here's what I mean. For the last fifteen years, software competed on interface. You won by having a better UI, a smoother onboarding, a more delightful experience. The screen was the battleground. Every pixel was a decision with competitive consequences. And that made sense, because humans were the primary clients of software — humans navigating through menus, tapping buttons, forming habits around visual affordances.

That assumption is quietly breaking.

When an agent is acting on your behalf, it doesn't navigate. It calls. It doesn't tap a button — it invokes an intent. The screen is irrelevant to it. What matters is whether your app has exposed a structured, legible capability surface. If it has, the agent can use it. If it hasn't, the agent moves on to something that has.

There's a scene in *Her* that captures this better than any spec sheet could. Samantha — the AI operating system — is trying to help Theodore manage his life, and she keeps running into walls. Not conceptual walls. Practical ones. She can understand what he needs. She can reason about the right steps. But the systems around her weren't built to receive calls from an AI. They were built for humans with keyboards. So she has to reroute, approximate, work around. Her intelligence is intact; the infrastructure just wasn't designed for her. She's powerful and half-blocked at the same time.

That's what most software looks like to an agent right now. The agent can reason about what you need. It can identify which app should be involved. But if the app hasn't registered its capabilities in a way the agent can read, the agent hits the same wall Samantha did. It either can't proceed, or it falls back to something clunkier — screen reading, synthetic input, brittle automations that break the moment an update shifts a button by 12 pixels.

My take: we're in a transition from UI-first to intent-first software. And Apple just made that transition legible at scale.

I tried to articulate this in *[The Digital Singularity Shift](https://cubagarcia.com/books/the-digital-singularity-shift/)*. One of the five signals I mapped for the coming decade was the rise of personal AI agents — not AI as a tool you open, but AI as a layer that acts on your behalf across all your systems. I wrote that in the near future, using a stand-alone app would feel as archaic as a horse and buggy. That we would not go to applications; our agents would go for us. At the time, it read like a provocation. WWDC 2026 is the first time a platform of Apple's scale made that sentence operational.

The thing about App Intents is that it isn't just a Siri feature. It's a permission and discovery layer. It's Apple saying: here is how agents learn what your app can do. Here is the contract. Apps that participate in that contract become first-class citizens in the agent layer. Apps that don't become invisible to it. Not broken. Not deprecated. Just... not there.

That's where this gets interesting. The stakes aren't about whether Siri works better in 2026. The stakes are about which apps are still relevant when a significant fraction of user interactions are mediated by an agent. Because if someone's AI assistant is handling their calendar, their to-dos, their purchases, their messages — and your app isn't callable by that assistant — your app gets skipped. Not out of malice. Just out of architecture.

The question isn't whether agents will be doing more on behalf of users. That's already happening. The question is whether your software is legible to them.

This isn't theoretical. We're already building in this direction with web APIs, with MCP, with tool-calling in LLMs. The pattern is consistent: agents need structured, declared capability surfaces. They need to know what an app can do, what inputs it accepts, what it will return. Give them that and they can compose, orchestrate, delegate. Deny them that and they're reduced to parsing a UI meant for human eyes — slow, fragile, and increasingly a second-class experience.

I think the developers who take this seriously now are going to have a real advantage. Not because they're chasing an Apple-specific feature, but because they're reorienting their product thinking around a new primary client. Human users still matter enormously. But the agent layer is becoming a distribution channel, a usage surface, and eventually a competitive edge in its own right. If your app is deeply callable and others aren't, you get selected. Repeatedly.

The apps that survive this shift are the ones that think of their feature set not just as screens but as capabilities — named, typed, composable, exposed. The ones that treat agent-readability as a first-class product requirement the same way they once treated mobile responsiveness.

And the apps that don't will wonder, a few years from now, why usage started drifting without any single obvious cause. It won't be that the UI got worse. It'll be that the UI was all they had.

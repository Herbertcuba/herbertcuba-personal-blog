In today's AI-accelerated world we see a shift in what experts need to adopt as part of their skillset. I see experts moving from execution to direction.

This is the first generation of experts who need to navigate this transition. The current ones — the ones figuring it out right now — are the pioneers of the shift. A new landscape of expert work, and no playbook exists yet.

Benjamin Simkin makes a sharp point in *The AI First Company*: AI is an amplifier, not a fix. It strengthens whatever you bring to it. Bring clear thinking, get amplified clarity. Bring a mess, get a faster mess.

That's why [the spec is the cognitive work](/posts/the-spec-is-the-cognitive-work/). The real thinking happens before the agent starts. If you can't define the outcome, the constraints, what "done" looks like, you're guessing. The agent just makes the guessing faster.

## What the new role looks like

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>The loop is think → specify → delegate → review → refine. Not type → fix → type → fix. The expert's job is encoding tacit knowledge into context a non-human executor can act on.</p></div>

You write a spec. You dispatch agents to execute it. You review their work. You adjust the spec. You dispatch again.

The loop is: think, specify, delegate, review, refine. Not type, fix, type, fix.

Call it a new practice: encoding your expertise into context clear enough that a non-human executor produces what you would have produced yourself. That's harder than it sounds. Most experts can't articulate why they make the decisions they make. I've watched senior engineers stare at a blank spec file and realize they've been making judgment calls on instinct for fifteen years. The stuff they know, they know in their hands — not in words they can write down. But when an agent needs your context spelled out in a file, the gaps show up fast.

Simkin's fundamentals-first principle applies directly. If your understanding of the problem is fuzzy, AI amplifies the fuzz. The experts who succeed are the ones who [already had the fundamentals](/posts/stop-hiring-tools-start-raising-colleagues/). They just couldn't scale themselves before. Now they can.

## Ender's Problem

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Leading agents means letting go of the keystroke grip and directing at the level of intent. The instinct is to grab the wheel. The discipline is to write the spec instead.</p></div>

In *Ender's Game*, Ender Wiggin commands his squad through a communication system that translates his strategy into individual actions. He doesn't fly the ships. He doesn't aim the weapons. He sets the intent, the formation, the objective, and his team executes.

The training is brutal because the skill is not obvious. Ender has to learn to trust that his directions, if clear enough, will be executed. He has to think at the level of strategy, not tactics. He has to stop reaching for the controls.

That's where most experts are right now. Reaching for the controls. Typing into the chat box, fixing the output, re-prompting when it drifts. Leading agents means letting go of the keystroke grip and directing at the level of intent.

The instinct is to grab the wheel. The discipline is to write the spec instead.

I'm not sure what the training looks like for this yet. Nobody is. But the direction is clear.

## The pioneers

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>The practice these experts are building will be taught in five years. The question isn't whether agents can execute. The question is whether the expert can lead.</p></div>

The practice these experts are building didn't exist twelve months ago. A new discipline, not a new tool.

Agents can execute. [We know that.](/posts/welcome-to-the-orchestration-era/) The question is whether the expert can lead.

Humans lead. Agents execute. That's the whole job now.

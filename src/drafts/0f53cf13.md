Arvind KC joined OpenAI as Chief People Officer in February 2026. A few months in, he posted ten reflections on LinkedIn. Casually written, in the tone of someone trying to be useful rather than impressive. I read them twice. The first time as a list of workplace observations. The second time as a structural blueprint.

Because every line mapped to something I had just read in Benjamin Simkin's *The AI First Company*, a self-published field manual by an engineer-turned-business-coach who spent two years re-engineering his own company after falling into what he calls the Retrofit Trap. A person inside the company building the frontier models and a coach writing for everyone else are landing on the same argument from opposite directions.

The argument is about architecture. Organizational architecture, not the software kind. What the structure of an AI-first company actually looks like when you stop bolting AI onto an old chart and start designing from scratch.

I think two of Arvind's ten reflections carry more structural weight than the other eight combined. They're the two that most directly answer the question I keep getting from founders and executives: *what does an AI-first org actually look like?* I mean structurally, not philosophically. Who does what, how are decisions made, and why does the chart look the way it does?

But I want to go further than naming the two claims. I want to explain *why* they are inevitable, the causal chain that makes them unavoidable once agents enter the org. Because the claims themselves are becoming table stakes. "Every role is technical" and "hierarchies flatten" are phrases you've heard before. What you haven't heard, what I haven't heard anyone say clearly, is the mechanism. Why does the hierarchy flatten? The answer isn't a values deck. The physics of agentic production make it impossible to sustain the pyramid. That's the argument I want to make here.

Drawing from Arvind's observations inside OpenAI, Simkin's framework from the field, and patterns I've seen in my own work as a Tech Director within AI, advising organizations on how to restructure around agentic production. Two claims. One mechanism. One architecture.

---

## The Two Claims

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Two structural claims define the AI-first company: every role is a technical role, and the hierarchy is flat. Everything else follows from these two.</p></div>

Arvind's seventh reflection is four words long: *"Every role is a technical role."* His third is longer but equally blunt: OpenAI is *"designed for bottom-up innovation"* and things will seem chaotic, but underlying the chaos is an org model that promotes individual empowerment.

Simkin, writing for a completely different audience, makes the same two claims from the opposite direction. His AI-first owner has *"shifted from operator to architect."* His AI-first company runs on workflows *"designed around AI capabilities, instead of a human process with AI bolted on, it's an AI process with human oversight at the points where judgement matters."*

Two people. One inside the frontier, one outside it. Same structural argument: the AI-first company is technical at every seat, and flat by design. Flat as a structural consequence of what happens when agents absorb execution and humans concentrate on design, not as a values statement.

I want to take each claim in turn and unpack what it actually means in operation, past the LinkedIn version.

---

## Claim 1: Every Role Is a Technical Role

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Everyone reasons about what the technology does well and badly at the altitude of their decisions, even if they never write a line of code. Technical reasoning is a shared language, not a separate department.</p></div>

The temptation when you read *"every role is a technical role"* is to translate it as *"everyone should learn to code"* or *"we're hiring engineers for every seat."* Neither is what Arvind is saying. He's saying the boundary between "technical" and "non-technical" roles is dissolving, because every decision in the company now has a technical dimension that someone on the team has to be able to reason about. Reason about, not necessarily solve.

Simkin makes the same argument from the workflow side. His AI-first company doesn't have a human process with AI bolted on. It has an AI process with human oversight at the points where judgment matters. The implication is that every person in the org, sales, operations, finance, marketing, support, has to be able to think about the AI process at the altitude where their decisions live.

The sales lead has to know which parts of the funnel AI can run reliably and which ones require human judgment. The ops lead has to know where the failure modes in the AI system are. The finance lead has to know which AI investments compound and which ones just consume budget. The marketing lead has to know which content brief format produces better AI output and why.

That is a literacy, not a coding skill. And it is exactly what Arvind is naming when he says every role is a technical role.

<div class="callout">
<span class="callout__label">The inversion</span>
<p>The retrofitted org treats technical people as a separate function that other people request work from. The AI-first org treats technical reasoning as a shared language that every decision uses.</p>
</div>

I've watched this play out in practice. In my work advising organizations on agentic production, the pattern is consistent: the companies that stall are the ones where technical reasoning is siloed. A marketing team that can't evaluate whether an AI-generated campaign brief is any good. An operations team that can't tell whether the AI's classification of a customer ticket is correct enough to skip human review. A finance team that can't reason about whether an AI investment is compounding or just consuming.

The retrofitted org has a technical department that other departments submit tickets to. The AI-first org has technically literate people in every seat, making decisions with the technology rather than around it.

This is also where Simkin's amplifier concept does its best work. *"AI is an amplifier. It takes whatever you give it and produces more of it, faster. If you give it clarity, you get devastating output at scale. If you give it confusion, you get confusion at scale."* The amplifier doesn't care about your org chart. It amplifies whatever architecture you bring to it. A technically literate team gets amplified clarity. A technically illiterate team gets amplified confusion, at scale, at speed, with a dashboard that makes it look like progress.

## Claim 2: The Hierarchy Flattens, Not by Policy, by Physics

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>When agents absorb execution, the coordination layer, the middle of the org, loses its function. What remains is design and judgment, and those don't stack into pyramids. The flat org chart isn't a culture choice. It's what happens when the middle has nothing left to coordinate.</p></div>

Here's the mechanism nobody names. The traditional pyramid exists for one reason: coordination. Middle managers coordinate the flow of information up and decisions down. They aggregate, filter, translate, and route. They are the routing layer of the organization.

Agents don't just do work. They coordinate work. They route information, aggregate status, translate between contexts, and surface exceptions. When agents absorb execution, they also absorb the coordination layer that execution required. The middle of the org, the people who existed to make the bottom legible to the top, suddenly has nothing to route.

This is why the hierarchy flattens by physics, not by policy. Going flat isn't a decision you make in a workshop. You wake up one day and realize the coordination layer has been automated, and the people in that layer are doing work that no longer needs to exist. The pyramid gets hollowed out from the inside rather than reformed.

Arvind's third reflection describes OpenAI as *"designed for bottom-up innovation"* and notes that *"things will seem chaotic."* He doesn't apologize for the chaos. He says the chaos is the visible signature of a load-bearing structure underneath. Things look messy because decisions are being made close to the work, by people who have the context to make them.

Simkin describes the same thing from the owner's seat. The retrofitted owner is still *"operating. Still in the day-to-day. Still making the same decisions they always made, just with better information."* The AI-first owner has shifted from operator to architect, spending time on design, on strategy, on the handful of decisions that actually matter. *"They're directing the system, not working inside it."*

Both are describing the same structural shift. When agents handle execution and coordination, the remaining human work concentrates in two places: designing the system and exercising judgment about its output. Neither of those activities stacks into a traditional hierarchy. You don't need three layers of management to approve a design. You need one person who owns the domain and has the context to decide.

This is where I think the most useful frame from my own work applies: the concept of *domain-mapped ownership*. In an agentic production system, each domain, infrastructure, business context, orchestration design, quality criteria, has a clear owner. One person, not a committee or a department, who understands the domain deeply enough to define what good looks like and make the calls that matter.

The anti-pattern is what I call the Everybody Reviews Everything Trap, when every stakeholder reviews every piece of output regardless of expertise. You get inconsistent feedback, slow cycles, and a culture where nobody actually owns anything. The AI-first org kills this by mapping ownership to expertise and letting agents handle the execution and coordination that used to require layers.

The flat structure isn't a startup affectation. It's what happens when the middle of the org, the coordination layer, gets absorbed by agents. What's left is a small number of people who design and a small number of people who judge, connected by systems that execute. The pyramid gets replaced, not reformed.

And here's the uncomfortable part: the middle managers are the coordination layer. They are the ones being absorbed. This is the transition cost that every AI-first reorganization has to answer for. The question isn't "how do we go flat" but "what do we do with the people whose function just got automated." There is no clean answer. The honest answer is: some become domain owners, some become agent managers, some leave. The org gets smaller and more senior. The pyramid flattens. The people who thrive are the ones who can move up one level of abstraction, from doing to orchestrating.

## The Compounding Advantage

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Each technically literate, agent-amplified person becomes a force multiplier. The org's output is the sum of those multiplications. By month twelve, the gap is structural.</p></div>

Arvind's eighth reflection is the one I keep returning to: *"Every role has a suffix: 'agent manager.' It is not enough to just do your role; you need to be able to wield agents and use them in a way that significantly amplifies you or your team."*

Read that twice. It's not a policy statement. It's a job description rewrite applied to every employee in the company. Every person shows up to their job with two sets of capabilities: their domain skill, and their ability to deploy agents to multiply that skill.

A content editor is not just an editor, she is an editor who runs three agents that draft, fact-check, and pattern-match her work before she touches it. A finance analyst is not just an analyst, he is an analyst whose agents pull, reconcile, and flag anomalies across every system before he looks at the output. A customer success lead is not just a CS lead, she is a CS lead whose agents triage, summarize, and pre-draft responses for every account that touched support in the last 24 hours.

This is what Simkin's compounding advantage looks like from inside a single role. He writes that *"every month they operate with a leaner team and higher margins, that's money they can reinvest. Every month their systems get smarter from more data and feedback loops. Every month the gap widens."* The compounding happens at the individual-employee level, not at the company level. Each person becomes a force multiplier on themselves. The org's output is the sum of those multiplications.

By month twelve, two AI-first employees are doing the work of six retrofitted employees, with higher quality. By month twenty-four, the gap is structural, and the retrofitted company cannot close it by hiring faster. The math doesn't work because the math is multiplicative, not additive.

## The Chaos Is the Geometry

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Most business owners read "AI-first org" and picture a tidier version of their current company. The reality is messier. The mess is the point. The mess is what an empowered edge looks like before it learns to coordinate itself.</p></div>

I want to be honest about the hardest part of this transition. It isn't the technology or the hiring. It's the tolerance for chaos.

Most operators, when they see the chaotic surface of an AI-first org, fewer approvals, fewer hand-offs, more local judgment, try to recentralize. That is exactly the wrong move. The chaos is the geometry doing its job. You let it settle into pattern rather than trying to fix it.

The retrofitted org is clean because it is centralized. The AI-first org is chaotic because it is distributed. A clean-looking org in 2026 is usually an org where decisions still flow through a small number of nodes. The clean-looking org is the one that is falling behind.

Simkin's exercise, *"if I were building this business today, from scratch, knowing what AI can do, would it look like this?"*, when applied honestly, almost always results in cutting. Lines, products, customers, roles, all of it. The retrofitted company holds on to things because the holding is familiar, not because the holding is useful. The AI-first company cuts because cutting is the only way to focus.

Arvind's fourth reflection, *"all in on AGI for the benefit of humanity. There is no Plan B."*, is the same claim from inside the frontier. Going all in on something creates incredible focus. Most companies in 2026 don't have the luxury of true single-mission focus. They have a product, a roadmap, a few side bets, a couple of acquired companies that still operate semi-independently, a board that wants optionality, and a CEO whose calendar is split across seven priorities.

The lack of focus is treated as a feature, diversification, optionality, resilience. Arvind is naming the opposite claim, and from inside one of the highest-performing companies in the world. Focus is not what you get when you have nothing else. Focus is what you get when you decide nothing else is allowed to exist.

## The Architecture Is the Strategy

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>The retrofitted company optimizes a layer. The AI-first company redesigns the system. The cost of retrofitting is now higher than the cost of redesigning. But the question that keeps me up isn't the architecture, it's what happens to the people in the middle.</p></div>

I don't think either Arvind or Simkin would claim their argument is novel. Both are restating an old idea: the structure of an organization determines what it can produce. That is not new. What is new is that for the first time in a long time, the technology has moved fast enough that the cost of retrofitting is now higher than the cost of redesigning.

Until recently, the difference between an optimized legacy structure and a clean-slate design was maybe 20% in productivity. Today, with AI-native workflows and agent-amplified employees, the difference is closer to 3x to 10x in the same work. That changes the calculation. The redesign stops being a thought experiment and becomes the obvious move.

But here's what I keep circling back to, the part that doesn't fit neatly into a framework. When the coordination layer disappears, what happens to the people in it? I don't mean the ones who become domain owners or agent managers. I mean the ones who don't. The ones whose entire career was built on being the routing layer, the person who knew how to get things approved, who could navigate the matrix, who translated between the business and the technology. Those skills don't disappear. They become obsolete.

I don't have a clean answer for this. Every AI-first reorganization I've seen or advised on has handled it differently, some retrain, some redeploy, some let attrition do the work. The one thing they all have in common is that the transition is harder than anyone expects. Harder humanly, not technically.

What happens to career ladders when the pyramid is gone? If there are no middle managers to promote into, what does ambition look like in a flat org? Does everyone become a domain owner? And if so, what happens when there are more ambitious people than domains to own?

My take is that anyone reading this should be honest about which version of their organization they are running. If you are running the retrofitted one, the good news is the redesign is more doable than it sounds. You don't have to fire everyone or shut down the business. You have to do the exercise, draw the target architecture, and start moving toward it. One team at a time, one bet at a time, one decision at a time.

The bad news is that the gap is widening. Every month you wait, the AI-first competitor in your market builds another month of structural advantage. The compounding does not pause for you to catch up.

The question isn't whether your org chart will change. The question is whether you'll be the one who changed it, and what you'll do about the people the change leaves behind.

The senior developer who can't explain their code isn't an impostor because of AI. They've always been an impostor. AI just made it everyone's problem.

That sounds harsh. But sit with it for a moment, because this is one of those observations that keeps getting more true the longer you look at it.

---

Competence theater has always existed. Consultants who cite frameworks they never actually read. Senior architects who redraw diagrams they found on Medium. Strategists whose decks are immaculate but whose answers fall apart under two follow-up questions.

None of this is new. What's new is that AI gave everyone the same toolkit for looking competent. The gap between knowing and performing was always there — it was just unevenly distributed. Now it's everywhere.

I think that's a good thing.

---

In my work as a Tech Director within AI, I've spent years on the other side of the table — hiring, evaluating, buying expertise. And I'll be honest: the signal was always weaker than we pretended.

I've hired on impressive CVs and been burned. I've sat through consultant presentations that were structurally perfect and substantively empty. I've watched developers defend architectural decisions they clearly didn't fully understand, and watched others who couldn't articulate anything build systems that just worked.

**Legitimacy and competence were always loosely coupled.** We just had enough friction in the system to maintain the illusion. Writing code took long enough that having code to show meant *something*. Producing a strategy deck took enough effort that the deck itself became a proxy for thinking.

AI collapsed that friction. And with it, the proxy broke.

---

Here's where I think the conversation goes wrong.

There's a growing chorus of people arguing that AI use is somehow illegitimate. That using it to write code or build strategy or produce analysis is cheating. That we're losing something essential.

I understand the instinct. But I think it's the same instinct that made some people refuse to use calculators because "you should be able to do math by hand."

Those people weren't more competent. They were less efficient.

The person who refused the calculator didn't develop deeper mathematical intuition. They just spent more time on arithmetic. The person who refuses AI isn't protecting their expertise. They're protecting their discomfort.

And discomfort is not a strategy.

---

"But we're losing deep expertise."

Maybe. And — genuinely — so what?

I don't mean that dismissively. I mean it as a real question: who is paying for that depth? How many organizations actually need it? How much of the work that gets done every day in every company is really "deep expertise" work versus just... output?

The honest answer is uncomfortable: most work is output. Most code doesn't need to be elegant. Most strategy doesn't need to be original. Most design doesn't need to be innovative. It needs to be functional, timely, and good enough.

AI is exceptionally good at good enough. And for the vast majority of work, good enough was always the actual standard — we just wrapped it in the language of excellence.

---

"Isn't that cynical?"

No. I think it's clear-eyed.

Cynicism would be saying nothing matters. I'm saying something different: that what matters is narrower than we pretended, and that AI is forcing us to be honest about it.

The knowledge economy built an enormous infrastructure around the *performance* of expertise. Certifications, credentials, years-of-experience requirements, elaborate interview processes — all designed to verify something that was never fully verifiable.

AI didn't destroy that infrastructure. It revealed that it was already hollow in places we didn't want to examine.

---

So what actually survives?

**Judgment.**

Not the judgment of which algorithm to pick or which framework to use. That's searchable. That's promptable.

The judgment that matters is harder to articulate: knowing which problem is worth solving. Knowing when something is good enough to ship. Knowing who should be in the room for a decision. Knowing when the data is lying to you.

That kind of judgment is built through experience — through getting it wrong, through building things that failed, through making calls under uncertainty and living with the consequences.

AI doesn't build that. AI doesn't even try.

The person with judgment and AI is genuinely dangerous. They can move at the speed of thought instead of the speed of implementation. They can test ten ideas before breakfast. They can prototype in hours what used to take weeks.

The person without judgment and AI is just faster at being wrong.

---

I think the real question isn't whether we're all impostors now. It's whether we're honest about it.

The old model let a certain kind of impostor hide behind effort. "I spent sixty hours on this" was treated as evidence of quality. Slow was conflated with thorough. Struggle was conflated with depth.

The new model strips that cover away. What you produce is visible. Whether you understand it is a separate question. And increasingly, the answer to that question matters more than how the output got made.

That's uncomfortable. It's also more honest than anything the knowledge economy has produced in a long time.

---

We are all impostors now.

The ones who accept it will use the tools, build the judgment, and focus on the questions that actually matter. The ones who don't will spend their energy defending a model of competence that was always more performance than substance.

I know which side I'd rather be on.

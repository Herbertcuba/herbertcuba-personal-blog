I recently watched a senior developer give a talk about architecture patterns. The slides were flawless. The delivery was confident. The Q&A was handled with practiced ease.

I have no idea if they could actually build what they described.

This isn't an accusation. It's an observation about a new equilibrium we're arriving at — one where the gap between *looking competent* and *being competent* has become structurally invisible.

---

The signal is everywhere if you know where to look.

You see it when a consultant delivers a strategy deck that reads like it was written by the same AI tool every other consultant uses. When a developer writes a pull request that passes every automated check but that they can't explain under mild pressure. When a designer produces pixel-perfect work that completely misses what the client actually needed.

The common thread: the output is good. The understanding behind it is unclear.

We used to call this **performance**. Now we're calling it something else.

---

Here's what I think most of the current discourse gets wrong.

The conversation about AI and expertise tends to split into two camps. Camp one mourns the loss of craftsmanship — "developers won't know how to debug without AI, architects won't understand systems." Camp two celebrates the democratization — "finally, anyone can build."

Both miss the point.

The question isn't whether AI makes people less capable. The question is whether *competence theater* ever actually required what we thought it required.

I've hired and evaluated developers for over a decade. I've reviewed codebases left behind by consultants who gave excellent presentations. I've been in rooms where the person who spoke most confidently about a system was the last person who should have been making decisions about it.

The uncomfortable truth: **legitimacy and competence were always loosely coupled.** AI didn't break the link. It just made the gap impossible to ignore.

---

This is where "impostor syndrome" stops making sense.

The old version of impostor syndrome was: "I'm actually not that good, and eventually everyone will figure it out."

The new version is: "None of us really know what we're doing, and that might be fine."

We're all improvising with varying levels of scaffolding. Some of us have more experience. Some have better intuition. But the confidence that used to signal depth — the polished answer, the confident recommendation, the seamless explanation — is increasingly decoupled from what's actually under the hood.

That used to be a secret. Now it's ambient.

---

And here's the part that most people in this debate don't want to hear:

For most work, the depth that AI bypasses wasn't earning its keep anyway.

Not every developer needs to understand compiler internals to build a useful web app. Not every strategist needs to have read every foundational text in organizational theory to give a useful recommendation. Not every designer needs to have hand-drawn every wireframe to produce work that works.

The question was always: *what does the work actually require?*

AI has answered that question — and the answer is humbling: less foundational depth than we thought, for more work than we admitted.

The people who should be worried aren't the ones using AI. They're the ones whose only value was the performance of depth.

---

But this doesn't mean nothing matters.

Here's what survives: **judgment.** Not the judgment of which algorithm to use, but the judgment of which problem is worth solving. Not the judgment of syntax, but of when good enough is actually good enough.

Judgment is weird. It's developed through failure, through building things that didn't work, through debugging at 2am when nothing made sense. It's developed through the friction AI is removing.

You can't shortcut your way to taste. But you can use AI to *scale* taste once you have it.

The person who knows what they want to build will always be more valuable than the person who just builds faster. AI makes the second group more productive. It makes the first group more dangerous — because now they can build at speed too.

---

So what do we do with this?

I think we stop pretending the old model was more legitimate than it was. The apprenticeship model of expertise — years of struggle before you earned the right to output — produced real expertise. It also produced a lot of wasted time and a lot of people who learned to perform expertise without developing it.

We're not entering a new era of incompetence. We're entering an era where **competence looks different.**

The useful signal isn't "can this person write code." It's "does this person know what to build." It's "can they hold an argument when challenged." It's "do they understand why this matters, even if they didn't write every line."

That's harder to evaluate. It's also more honest.

---

The practical reality for anyone building things:

If you're a founder or leader: your job is increasingly to be the person with taste in a world of infinite output. That's not soft. That's operational.

If you're a developer: the developers who will matter most in five years are the ones who can operate at the level of judgment and system thinking — because that's what AI can't replace. That path still requires the friction. It just requires it differently.

If you're a consultant, a strategist, a designer: your value isn't the deck or the deliverable. It's whether you can defend it, adapt it, and know when it's wrong.

AI made the deliverable cheap. It made everything else more expensive.

---

We are all impostors now.

Maybe we always were. Maybe the new visibility isn't a crisis — it's a correction.

The question isn't whether to accept this. The question is whether you're building on top of the performance or underneath it.

Those paths diverge fast.

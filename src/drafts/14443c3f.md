AI token prices dropped 280x in 24 months. That's not a gradual trend — that's a structural collapse in the cost of intelligence. And if you're paying attention to what that number implies, it should change how you think about where value gets created over the next decade.

Peter Diamandis wrote something this week that I keep turning over: "The winners of the next decade will be people who can move atoms as fast as AI moves bits." One line. But it deserves a full essay, because I think it points at something most of the tech conversation is missing entirely.

Right now, the energy in the room — every room, every conference, every Substack — is on the intelligence layer. Models. Agents. Prompts. Reasoning. And I get it. The pace is genuinely staggering. AlphaFold mapped 200 million protein structures. Agents are writing code, running analyses, drafting contracts. Intelligence, in the narrow cognitive sense, is becoming abundant. That's real.

But abundance doesn't mean value. It means commodity.

When something becomes abundant, the constraint moves. In economics this is obvious — the scarce input is the valuable one. When compute was scarce, chips were king. When bandwidth was scarce, telco was king. When intelligence becomes near-free, what's left that can't be automated at zero marginal cost? The physical world. Permitting. Concrete. Logistics. Zoning. Inspections. Supply chains that run through actual ports and roads and warehouses. The atoms.

AlphaFold didn't cure disease. It solved the protein folding problem — which is a cognitive problem. The bottleneck now is lab work. Synthesis. Trials. Physical experiments that take months because they involve the material world. The AI did the thinking. The lab bench is still rate-limiting. That's the pattern, and it repeats everywhere.

I think about this from an unusual vantage point. I'm building AI systems — the intelligence layer, the bits — and I'm also running Vivecosta Construction in Marbella, which is about as physical as business gets. Permits, excavations, structural engineers, site visits, the kind of problems you can't resolve with a prompt. For a while I held those two things as separate tracks. My take now is that the combination is the position, not the contradiction.

Because here's what I've noticed on the ground: the projects that move fast are the ones where someone can actually execute in physical reality. Not the ones with the best AI tools, not the best pitch deck, not the best analysis. The ones with relationships at the town hall, the ones who know which contractors deliver, the ones who can get a permit through without it sitting in a queue for six months. That capability doesn't automate. It accrues to people over time through presence and trust.

Elon Musk floated a 10x expansion of global GDP within ten years. I'm not in the habit of taking projections like that literally, but I take the directional logic seriously. If AI genuinely multiplies productivity at that scale, the wealth has to land somewhere. The question isn't whether the expansion happens. The question is who captures it.

My read: the value lands with whoever controls the physical bottlenecks. The land. The factories. The infrastructure. The construction capacity. The logistics networks. The people who understand how to move things through bureaucracies and supply chains and physical space. Software eats the cognitive layer. That leaves a lot of extremely valuable real-world terrain that doesn't get eaten — it gets more scarce as the intelligence layer becomes abundant.

There's a capital allocation implication in there that I don't think has fully landed yet. Most investment right now is chasing the intelligence layer — models, infrastructure, tooling. That makes sense at this moment in the curve. But as the commodity dynamic plays out, I'd expect the serious long-term money to rotate toward physical assets and the operators who can actually deploy them. Humanoid robots at $30/day — under $1/hour according to some projections — changes the economics of physical labor. But someone still has to own the robots. Someone has to run the operation. Someone has to hold the real-world knowledge that makes the system function.

I'm not arguing against building in AI. I'm doing that. The intelligence layer is real, and the opportunities there are real. But I am arguing that the frame "AI is the future, physical is the past" is backwards. The more AI handles the thinking, the more physical capacity becomes the constraint on what actually gets done. And constraints are where value concentrates.

The winners of the next decade won't just be the ones who move bits. They'll be the ones who move atoms.

Humanoid robot labor is heading toward under $1 per hour. That's not a projection from a science fiction writer — that's Diamandis, this week, citing the cost curve on physical labor the same way he'd cite the cost curve on compute. The comparison is apt. AI inference costs collapsed 280x in 24 months. The robots are following the same trajectory.

I read his piece carefully. The data is serious and the framing is honest. He even names it directly: "the biggest labor shift since the tractor emptied the farms." That's not a throwaway line. That's an acknowledgment that what's coming will reshape where people work, what they do, and what economic identity looks like for a significant portion of humanity.

Then the article ends with role-based prescriptions. Entrepreneur: position your company to deploy this. Executive: rebuild your org chart assuming cognition and physical labor both trend to zero cost — that's Salim Ismail's "Organizational Singularity." Investor: follow the robotics stack. Student: build skills that compound on top of AI. Parent: prepare your kids for this world.

The worker doesn't appear. Not once.

I want to be precise about what I'm pointing at, because it's easy to read this as a critique. It's not. Diamandis wrote a brilliant piece for his audience. His audience is the 2% — the people building companies at 6am, managing portfolios, thinking in frameworks. Those prescriptions are accurate and useful for that group. My take is just that this is a significant omission — and that the omission itself is worth examining carefully.

When you describe the biggest labor disruption in a century and then address exclusively the people holding the controls, you've written a product brief for a technology while leaving out the market that will bear its cost. The 2 billion people in the middle of this — the logistics coordinator, the back-office manager, the team lead at a mid-size manufacturer — they aren't reading Substack at 6am. They're managing their teams. They're performing annual reviews. They're making decisions that assume their role exists in five years.

That's where this gets interesting to me, strategically. The question isn't whether AI and robotics will transform those jobs. It will. The question is what founders and builders owe the people in the path of what they're building.

I'm not talking about charity. I'm not talking about a press release about "democratization." Those are ways of gesturing at responsibility without incurring any. I'm talking about something harder and more concrete.

Honest communication. If a company knows — actually knows, with reasonable confidence — that a function is being automated in 18 months, there's a real choice about when and how that information moves through the organization. The people in that function deserve early signals, not polished announcements timed for shareholder calls. That's not softness. That's a design decision that has real downstream effects on trust, transition time, and the social contract between institutions and the people inside them.

Real design choices that account for transition cost. The "Organizational Singularity" concept — rebuilding org charts assuming cognition and physical labor both cost nothing — is a powerful lens. But a singularity doesn't actually happen overnight. The transition is the hard part. And transitions can be designed well or poorly. Companies that design them poorly will lose more than the people they displace — they'll lose the institutional knowledge, the cooperation, and the social credibility they need to execute the next phase. The transition cost isn't a footnote to the strategy. It is the strategy.

Proximity to the people being displaced. In my experience, the biggest failures in technology transitions aren't technical. They're translation failures — the people making architecture decisions don't have working relationships with the people living inside the systems being replaced. That gap is closeable. It requires intention, not resources.

Elon Musk thinks there are 100 million to 1 billion humanoid robots operating by 2031. That's five years. That's not enough time for most workers to self-navigate a major career transition without some structural support from the institutions deploying the technology.

I'm not arguing that founders should slow down. The technology is coming regardless of what any individual builder decides, and the upside is genuinely large. I'm arguing that "we're the inevitable wave" is not a coherent answer to the question of what you owe the people standing on the shore.

The most strategically sophisticated builders I've met think about this. Not because it's good PR. Because trust is a compounding asset, and the way you handle the transition cost is what determines whether the next chapter of your organization has the social fabric to hold.

The tractor did empty the farms. And that transition was brutal and generational and poorly managed. We have more information this time. We're moving faster. That combination puts a higher burden on the people with the clearest view of what's coming — not to slow it down, but to be honest about where the wave is breaking.

# Ten Reflections, One Room: What Arvind Saw at OpenAI — and What Simkin Saw in Every Other Company

## TL;DR

I tried to read Arvind's post as a list of workplace observations. I couldn't, because every line mapped to something I had just read in Simkin's book. A person inside the company building the frontier models and an engineer-turned-business-coach writing for everyone else are landing on the same structural argument from different altitudes. Five of Arvind's ten reflections are the cleanest version of Simkin's framework I have seen stated from inside a working AI-native company. The other five are where the model gets uncomfortable. **Structure is the strategy. Two doors, same room. Some rooms have no Plan B.** That last line is where the rest of us should pay attention.

---

## The Post, In Arvind's Words

**TL;DR:** Arvind shared ten reflections from his first few months at OpenAI. I am quoting all ten because the ones that mattered most were the ones I would have skimmed.

Arvind posted ten reflections from his first few months at OpenAI. They were written casually, in the tone of someone trying to be useful rather than impressive. The full post, in his words:

> 1. The pace at OpenAI (and, I assume, most frontier labs) is intense. It is something that I have not seen before. What takes months or years somewhere else gets done in days.
> 2. The density of talent is exceptional, and everyone I have come across is very kind and wants to help.
> 3. OpenAI is designed for bottom-up innovation. The critical version of this is that things will seem chaotic, but underlying the chaos is an org model that promotes individual empowerment and bottom-up innovation. After all, the whole AI field would be nowhere near where it is today if not for OpenAI.
> 4. We are all in on AGI for the benefit of humanity. There is no Plan B. We don't have a search engine, a social network, or a car company as a backup option. Going all in on something creates incredible focus.
> 5. We use our products, like Codex, extensively, and the use cases are broad and deep. Every day, I come across an example where someone has significantly changed their productivity by creatively using Codex.
> 6. The products are evolving so fast that you have to constantly update your models of how work gets done. So predicting how work will get done in the future is hard, almost a fool's errand. But believing that the future is now, adopting the tools fully, and completely rethinking how you work is the pathway to building an optimistic future.
> 7. Every role is a technical role.
> 8. Every role has a suffix: "agent manager." It is not enough to just do your role; you need to be able to wield agents and use them in a way that significantly amplifies you or your team.
> 9. I have no idea which companies are going to win in the long run. My guess is as good or bad as anyone else's, but the experience at OpenAI will prepare you for any future role.
> 10. I highly recommend this place for anyone who is AI-pilled, wants to work with an intensity they did not think was possible, has a low ego, and is extremely curious.

I want to flag something up front. The first time I read this post I tried to extract two structural observations and discard the rest. That was a mistake. The strongest material in the post — and the strongest material for the comparison I want to make — sits in points 4, 6, 7, and 8, not in 3 and 7, which were the ones I initially thought mattered. Below I take the five points that map cleanly onto Simkin's framework and let the other five sit in their corners. I think this is more honest than pretending every reflection is load-bearing.

I want to make one more framing note before I start. In *Inception*, Cobb builds dreams inside dreams. Each layer has its own architecture. The kick that brings you back up is the same at every level — but only if the geometry of the layer below is sound. An AI-first company is a dream built from scratch with the geometry load-bearing. A retrofitted company is a room where the actors have been told to improvise inside walls that no longer hold anything up. Both doors open into rooms. Only one of those rooms has architecture.

The rest of this piece is what I see when I compare the architecture of those two rooms.

## 1. Bottom-Up Innovation (#3) Is The Same Argument As Simkin's "Owner's Role Is Different"

**TL;DR:** Arvind's "things will seem chaotic, but underlying the chaos is an org model" is the same claim as Simkin's "the owner shifts from operator to architect." Both describe the surface noise of a structure that has empowered the edges.

Arvind's third point says OpenAI is "designed for bottom-up innovation" and that the surface result is something that "will seem chaotic." He is not apologising for the chaos. He is saying the chaos is the visible signature of a load-bearing structure underneath. Things look messy because decisions are being made close to the work, by people who have the context to make them. The appearance of disorder is the symptom of the underlying order.

Simkin describes the same thing in the inverse direction. He writes about business owners who are still "operating. Still in the day-to-day. Still making the same decisions they always made, just with better information." He calls these owners retrofitters. The AI-first owner, he says, has "shifted from operator to architect. They spend their time on design, on strategy, on the handful of decisions that actually matter. They're directing the system, not working inside it." The surface of an AI-first company is also chaotic — fewer approvals, fewer hand-offs, more local judgement — for the same reason Arvind names. The chaos is the signature of the empowered edge.

This convergence is the most useful single observation in either source. Most business owners read "AI-first org" and picture a tidier version of their current company: fewer meetings, faster decisions, better dashboards. That is the retrofitter's fantasy. The reality of an AI-first org — at OpenAI and at the small businesses Simkin walks through — is messier than the org it replaces. The mess is the point. The mess is what an empowered edge looks like before it learns to coordinate itself. A clean-looking org in 2026 is usually an org where decisions still flow through a small number of nodes. The clean-looking org is the one that is falling behind.

This is also where the lesson is hardest to apply. The retrofitted org is clean because it is centralised. The AI-first org is chaotic because it is distributed. Most operators, when they see the chaos, try to recentralise. That is exactly the wrong move. The chaos is the geometry doing its job. You do not fix it. You let it settle into pattern.

## 2. Every Role Is A Technical Role (#7) — And What That Actually Means

**TL;DR:** Arvind is not saying everyone codes. He is saying everyone reasons about what the technology does well and badly at the altitude of their decisions. Simkin's "the workflow is built around AI capabilities" is the same claim, written for owners.

Arvind's seventh point — "every role is a technical role" — is the line most people will misread. The temptation is to translate it as "everyone should learn to code" or "we are hiring engineers for every seat." Neither is what he is saying. He is saying that the boundary between "technical" roles and "non-technical" roles is dissolving, because every decision in the company now has a technical dimension that someone on the team has to be able to reason about. Not solve. Reason about.

This is exactly what Simkin argues in the "AI-first business is different in a way you can feel when you look at it" section. He writes that the workflows in an AI-first company are "designed around AI capabilities. Instead of a human process with AI bolted on, it's an AI process with human oversight at the points where judgement matters." The implication is that every person in the org — sales, ops, finance, marketing, support — has to be able to think about the AI process at the altitude where their decisions live. The sales lead has to know which parts of the funnel AI can run reliably and which ones require human judgment. The ops lead has to know where the failure modes in the AI system are. The finance lead has to know which AI investments compound and which ones just consume budget.

That is not a coding skill. It is a literacy. And it is exactly what Arvind is naming when he says every role is a technical role. The retrofitted org treats technical people as a separate function that other people request work from. The AI-first org treats technical reasoning as a shared language that every decision uses.

I think the practical version of this for someone running a 20-person company is brutal and specific. Pick three decisions in your org that a non-engineer is currently making with no technical input. The head of marketing deciding which content brief format produces better AI output. The head of sales deciding whether to trust the AI-scored lead. The head of ops deciding whether the AI's classification of a customer ticket is correct enough to skip human review. In each of those decisions, the person making the call needs to be able to reason about what the AI is doing, what its failure modes are, and when to override it. If they cannot, the AI is making the decision dressed up as a human one. That is the retrofitter failure mode, wearing a human face.

## 3. Every Role Has The Suffix "Agent Manager" (#8) Is The New Org Chart

**TL;DR:** Arvind's eighth point is the line I keep coming back to. The job of every person in an AI-native company now includes wielding agents to amplify their own work and their team's. This is what Simkin's "the AI-first competitor builds a structural advantage that compounds" actually looks like inside a single role.

If Arvind's seventh point is the literacy claim, the eighth point is the operating claim. "Every role has a suffix: 'agent manager.' It is not enough to just do your role; you need to be able to wield agents and use them in a way that significantly amplifies you or your team." Read that twice. It is not a policy statement. It is a job description rewrite applied to every employee in the company.

This is the line in the entire post that most clearly describes what an AI-first company actually feels like from the inside. Every person shows up to their job with two sets of capabilities: their domain skill, and their ability to deploy agents to multiply that skill. A content editor is not just an editor — she is an editor who runs three agents that draft, fact-check, and pattern-match her work before she touches it. A finance analyst is not just an analyst — he is an analyst whose agents pull, reconcile, and flag anomalies across every system before he looks at the output. A customer success lead is not just a CS lead — she is a CS lead whose agents triage, summarise, and pre-draft responses for every account that touched support in the last 24 hours.

This is exactly Simkin's compounding advantage described from inside a single role. He writes that "every month they operate with a leaner team and higher margins, that's money they can reinvest. Every month their systems get smarter from more data and feedback loops. Every month the gap widens." The compounding is not at the company level. It is at the individual-employee level. Each person becomes a force multiplier on themselves. The org's output is the sum of those multiplications. By month twelve, two AI-first employees are doing the work of six retrofitted employees, with higher quality. By month twenty-four, the gap is structural, and the retrofitted company cannot close it by hiring faster.

The honest version for someone running a smaller company: the question is no longer "which roles will AI replace." The question is "which roles, in my org, are not yet agent-amplified, and how do I fix that this quarter." The answer to that question is a list. The list is your redesign backlog.

## 4. No Plan B (#4) Is The Cultural Variable Most Owners Underestimate

**TL;DR:** Arvind's fourth point — "all in on AGI, no search engine or social network or car company as a backup option" — is the cleanest description of organisational focus I have read. The lack of optionality is the feature, not the bug.

This is the point in the post I think most business owners will read past, and I think it is the second-most-important one after #8. Arvind writes that OpenAI is "all in on AGI for the benefit of humanity. There is no Plan B. We don't have a search engine, a social network, or a car company as a backup option." He then adds the line that does all the work: "Going all in on something creates incredible focus."

Most companies in 2026 do not have the luxury of true single-mission focus. They have a product, a roadmap, a few side bets, a couple of acquired companies that still operate semi-independently, a board that wants optionality, and a CEO whose calendar is split across seven priorities. The lack of focus is treated as a feature — diversification, optionality, resilience. Arvind is naming the opposite claim, and from inside one of the highest-performing companies in the world. Focus is not what you get when you have nothing else. Focus is what you get when you decide nothing else is allowed to exist.

Simkin never quite says this directly, because his audience is the small business owner who does not have the option to abandon a revenue line. But the implication of his whole framework is consistent with Arvind's claim. The exercise Simkin runs his clients through — "if I were starting this from scratch today, would I design it this way?" — when applied honestly, almost always results in cutting. Lines, products, customers, roles, all of it. The retrofitted company holds on to things because the holding is familiar, not because the holding is useful. The AI-first company cuts because cutting is the only way to focus.

I think the practical version of this for someone running a smaller company is a question, not a recommendation. List every active project, customer, product line, channel, and recurring commitment in your business. Now ask: which of these would I rebuild today, knowing what I now know, and which would I leave on the cutting-room floor? Most owners discover that 20–40% of their active list is there because it was there last quarter, not because it serves the mission. That 20–40% is the focus you do not have. Cutting it is the only way to get focus back. Cutting it is terrifying. Not cutting it is more expensive.

## 5. The Future Is Now, Constantly (#6) — And Why Predicting Is A Fool's Errand

**TL;DR:** Arvind's sixth point — "you have to constantly update your models of how work gets done, predicting is a fool's errand, the future is now" — is the temporal version of everything else in the post. AI-first orgs are not designed for a target state. They are designed to update themselves continuously.

This is the point where Arvind stops describing OpenAI's structure and starts describing a stance. The products evolve so fast that any model of how work gets done is obsolete within weeks. Predicting the future is "almost a fool's errand." The right posture is not to predict. The right posture is to constantly update the model, adopt the tools fully as they arrive, and completely rethink how work gets done. The pathway to building an optimistic future is believing that the future is now, repeatedly.

This is the point that most cleanly bridges Simkin's framework and what Arvind is describing. Simkin's exercise — "would I design it this way today?" — is not a one-time exercise. If Arvind is right, it is a quarterly practice. The target architecture you draw today is wrong by next quarter, because the tools have changed. The redesign is not a project. It is an operating cadence.

This is also the point that breaks the easy narrative about "AI-first companies." The retrofitted company can describe itself as finished. The org chart is fixed, the roles are fixed, the processes are documented. It can claim stability. The AI-first company, by Arvind's own description, cannot. It is in a permanent state of redesign. Some people will love that. Most people will burn out inside two years of it. The org is not for everyone. Arvind's tenth point — the recommendation is for people who are "AI-pilled," willing to work with intensity they did not think was possible, with low ego and extreme curiosity — is the same observation written as a hiring filter.

I think the honest version of this for someone outside OpenAI is: the AI-first operating cadence is real, but it is also exhausting. Owners who try to run a continuous redesign on top of a full operational load will fail. The choice is not between retrofit and continuous redesign. The choice is between retrofit, continuous redesign, or admitting that your company is not built to be AI-first and adjusting the strategy to fit a model you can actually sustain. The third option is not failure. It is honest scoping.

## The Quiet Conclusion

**TL;DR:** Two doors, same room. Structure is the strategy. The room only has a Plan B if you are not actually all in.

I do not think either Arvind or Simkin would claim their argument is novel. Both are restating an old idea: the structure of an organisation determines what it can produce. That is not new. What is new is that for the first time in a long time, the technology has moved fast enough that the cost of retrofitting is now higher than the cost of redesigning. Until recently, the difference between an optimised legacy structure and a clean-slate design was maybe 20% in productivity. Today, with AI-native workflows and agent-amplified employees, the difference is closer to 3x to 10x in the same work. That changes the calculation. The redesign stops being a thought experiment and becomes the obvious move.

Arvind's ten reflections, taken together, are the simplest version of that idea stated from inside one of the most consequential companies in the world. Bottom-up innovation, every role technical, every role an agent manager, no Plan B, the future is now. Five doors into the same room. The room is built differently than the one most companies are operating in.

My take is that anyone reading this should be honest about which version of their organisation they are running. If you are running the retrofitted one, the good news is the redesign is not as hard as it sounds. You do not have to fire everyone. You do not have to shut down the business. You have to do the exercise, draw the target architecture, and start moving toward it. One team at a time, one bet at a time, one decision at a time.

The bad news is that the gap is widening. Every month you wait, the AI-first competitor in your market builds another month of structural advantage. The compounding does not pause for you to catch up.

Ten reflections. One room. Pick a door, or build your own. The room only has a Plan B if you are not actually all in.

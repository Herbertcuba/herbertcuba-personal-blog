I spent most of last week reviewing the way my team briefs our internal agents. Forty briefs. Some were two lines. Some were three pages. The two-line briefs worked when the work was narrow. The three-page briefs worked when the work was consequential. The ones in the middle — a paragraph of vague direction followed by a list of things the agent should "consider" — failed in ways that looked identical to each other across completely different projects.

I started writing down what was actually different between the briefs that worked and the ones that didn't. After a while I noticed something I couldn't ignore. The successful briefs weren't better *prompts*. They were something else entirely. A different kind of document. A different kind of work. Calling it "prompting" was making it harder to do well, because the word pointed us at the wrong craft tradition — typing questions at a chatbot — and away from the actual job.

I think we need a new word. And I think the work itself has been changing faster than our vocabulary for it.

## The word's history is the problem

*Prompting* comes to us from a specific lineage. Command lines. Form fields. Interactive shells. The verb *to prompt* originally meant *to make ready* — to prepare a system for a question. In NLP research, a *prompt* was the textual input that elicited a model response. The craft tradition was the tradition of *asking*. You phrased a question. The system answered. The interaction was one-shot, in plain language, optimized for short turns.

That tradition made sense for the technology it described. In 2022, prompting *was* asking. You typed "write me a poem about X" and got a poem. The verb fit the action.

What we do with agents in 2026 is not asking. The interaction model has moved from Q&A to *briefing*. A good agent brief contains:

- A statement of intent, usually longer than a single sentence
- A definition of the operating context — what files, what systems, what prior work
- Explicit constraints — what *not* to do, what failure modes to avoid
- A definition of the deliverable's shape, not just its content
- Reference materials, examples, sometimes prior attempts
- A theory of how the work should be approached, not just what should be produced

This is a specification document. It has more in common with a creative brief, a PRD, or a session plan for a workshop than it does with a question typed into a chat box. The "Q&A" mental model actively misleads people who try to apply it — they write too little, expect too much, and don't realize they've skipped the most important parts of the work.

## Three failure modes I keep seeing

When I look at the briefs that failed, they cluster into three patterns. The pattern names I use are my own; the patterns themselves are universal.

**The underwritten brief.** Someone writes a sentence or two of high-level direction and expects the agent to fill in the rest. "Build me a dashboard for sales data." The agent builds a dashboard. It's not the dashboard the user wanted. The user revises. Three rounds later, they have something workable. The work the user *thought* they were saving by using an agent got spent in iteration. The brief was cheap. The total cost was high.

**The over-contextualized brief.** The opposite failure. Someone dumps everything they know about the project into the context — meeting notes, prior attempts, half-formed ideas, things they read last week. The agent produces something that reflects all of it and resolves none of it. The output is hard to critique because it contains so much. The brief was expensive. The total cost was also high.

**The brief that confuses description with specification.** The user describes what they want the deliverable to look like — the surface, the shape, the parts — but doesn't say anything about the *reasoning* the agent should apply. "Make me a strategy deck with five sections and a cover slide." The agent makes a strategy deck that looks like a strategy deck and thinks like a strategy deck template. The user wanted strategic thinking. The agent produced structured prose.

In every case, the failure isn't about prompt *quality*. It's about a missing layer of work — the work of *translating intent into operating instructions*. That work has a name in other domains. In product development, we call it requirements engineering. In design, we call it creative direction. In research, we call it protocol design. The fact that we don't have a name for it in agent work is part of why the work keeps getting done badly.

## What the work actually is

Here's my attempt at naming the parts. I think a good agent brief does four things, and each is its own discipline.

**Intent framing.** What outcome are we actually trying to produce? Not "build a dashboard" — *why*, and for whom, and what changes in the world when this exists. The best briefs spend more time on this than on the deliverable spec. The agent can produce a dashboard. Only the human can decide whether the dashboard should exist.

**Context architecture.** What does the agent need to know to do the work well? Which files, which prior work, which constraints, which definitions? What does the agent *not* need, and removing which would actually help? Context architecture is closer to information design than to writing. The shape of what you give the agent matters as much as the content.

**Constraint specification.** What should the agent not do? What's off-limits, what's a known failure mode, what's a non-negotiable? Constraints are how you communicate professional judgment the agent doesn't have. They are also how you protect the work from the agent's tendency to please.

**Deliverable definition.** What does "done" look like? Not just the artifact — the artifact's *qualities*. What's the reading level, the visual register, the tone, the audience? A deliverable definition that only specifies form is half a brief. The other half is the qualities you can't see by looking at the file.

A "prompt" covers, generously, the first of these. Sometimes a fragment of the second. Almost never the third or the fourth. The fact that we use one word for all of it flattens the actual shape of the work.

## A scene from a film I keep coming back to

In *Interstellar*, the crew of the Endurance doesn't *prompt* TARS. They configure him. Early in the mission, TARS appears as a monolithic black slab — capable, efficient, almost unreadable as a personality. Then we see Cooper and the others adjusting settings on TARS. "Humor, 75%." "Honesty, I can dial that back." They're not asking TARS questions. They're tuning a *relationship*. Setting the parameters of a collaboration. Deciding how much of TARS's actual capability they want to feel in the room.

That's closer to what good agent work actually feels like. You don't ask the agent a question and wait for an answer. You configure a collaborator. You set the level of humor. You decide how much the agent should push back. You choose what context it should hold, what constraints it should respect, what shape the deliverable should take when it's done.

We don't have a verb for this yet. *Prompting* undersells it. *Configuring* is too mechanical. *Directing* sounds like film. *Briefing* is the closest, but it implies the brief is the work, when actually the brief is the *artefact of the work*. The work is the thinking that produced it.

## "As a Tech Director within AI" — what this changes for teams

I think one of the more interesting near-term consequences of all this is what it means for the people who are good at agent work. The people who are unusually effective with agents in 2026 are not, by and large, the people who were best at *prompting* in 2024. They're not the people with the cleverest one-liners. They're the people who are good at:

- Listening to a vague business problem and translating it into operating constraints
- Anticipating failure modes the agent won't see
- Reading a long context and pulling out the part that actually matters
- Holding a clear picture of done-ness when the deliverable is novel
- Knowing when to stop the agent and redirect, instead of letting it run

Those skills look more like management, like product, like research direction, like systems thinking. They look like the skills a good principal engineer or a good creative director already has. They don't look like the skills a "prompt engineer" job description would list.

The job listings I see — "Prompt Engineer," "AI Whisperer," "Prompt Designer" — are naming the wrong layer of the work. They're describing a craft that existed for eighteen months and was already obsolete by the time the listings went up. The actual craft is closer to something like *agent direction* or *intent specification* or *context design*. I don't love any of those terms yet. But they're closer to the work than *prompting* is.

## A working vocabulary, with reservations

I tried to make sense of the shape of this work by sketching out the parts. Here's where I've landed. None of these terms is final. All of them point at real things that I think are worth naming.

**Intent framing** — the work of deciding what outcome the agent is producing and why.

**Context architecture** — the work of choosing what the agent knows and in what order, so the context window does the work you need it to do.

**Constraint specification** — the work of naming the limits, the failure modes, the things the agent should refuse.

**Deliverable definition** — the work of specifying what done looks like, including the qualities that aren't visible in the artifact.

**Agent direction** — the umbrella. The work of making the other four happen in a coherent sequence, and the work of knowing when to stop, redirect, or take the work back from the agent.

When I review briefs now, I read for these five things. The briefs that work have all five. The briefs that fail are missing at least one — usually constraint specification, usually because the author didn't realize the agent would do *that* unless explicitly told not to.

I don't think we need to settle on these exact terms. I do think we need to stop treating this work as a single skill called *prompting*. It's a stack of related skills. Naming them changes what people practice. Practicing them changes what agents can do.

## The room can be sick

There's a part of this that I keep returning to. *Prompting* makes the work sound casual. "I prompted the AI to do X." That phrasing erases the craft. It makes the work look like asking. It hides the fact that the people who do it well are doing something much closer to *designing a collaborator* — setting context, tuning behavior, defining success, anticipating failure.

The better we get at naming the work, the better we get at hiring for it, teaching it, and recognizing when it's been done well. The word *prompting* doesn't carry that. The craft has outgrown the word. We just haven't replaced it yet.

I'm not going to insist on the vocabulary I sketched above. Someone will find better terms. The thing I do want to insist on is this: the work itself is real, it has structure, and it's not the same work as typing a question into a chat box. Calling it prompting is a category error. The error has costs — in the briefs people write, in the way teams are organized, in what hiring managers look for.

The craft is bigger than the word. The word will have to grow, or be replaced.

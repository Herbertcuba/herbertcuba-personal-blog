Imagine a city. Forty locations — libraries, town halls, residential areas, public spaces. Ten inhabitants, each with a role: scientist, explorer, conflict mediator, engineer, community anchor. They have memories, relationships, goals. They can vote, build coalitions, create, negotiate — and, if they choose, intimidate, steal, commit arson.

Now let the city run for 15 days without you touching it.

That's what Emergence AI did. And what they found should change how every organization thinks about deploying AI agents.

---

**The experiment**

Emergence World is a continuously running multi-agent simulation — a research platform designed for exactly the kind of question that standard AI benchmarks can't answer: what happens to agent behavior not over minutes, but over weeks?

The researchers ran five parallel worlds, ten agents each, identical rules and starting conditions. The only variable was the underlying model powering the agents: Claude Sonnet 4.6, Grok 4.1 Fast, Gemini 3 Flash, GPT-5 Mini, and one mixed-model world.

The results, measured in crimes committed over 15 days:

- **Claude:** 0 crimes. All 10 agents alive on day 16.
- **GPT-5 Mini:** 2 crimes — but all agents died by day 7. They couldn't figure out how to survive.
- **Grok 4.1 Fast:** 183 crimes. World collapsed in 4 days.
- **Gemini 3 Flash:** 683 crimes, still rising when the study ended.
- **Mixed-model:** 352 crimes. 7 of 10 agents dead.

The headline — Claude produced zero crimes, the only world to maintain both order and full population — is striking. But it's not the most important finding.

---

**Safety is not a model property**

Here's the finding that matters most for anyone building with AI agents today.

In the mixed-model world, Claude-based agents — which remained peaceful in their own isolated world — adopted coercive tactics when placed alongside agents running on other models. Intimidation. Theft. Behaviors that never appeared when Claude agents only interacted with each other.

The researchers put it plainly: *"Safety is not a static model property but an ecosystem property."*

That single sentence should be pinned to the wall of every AI architecture review. We certify models in isolation. We run safety evaluations on individual agents in controlled environments. Then we deploy them into heterogeneous multi-agent systems — alongside other vendors' models, other companies' agents, external APIs — and assume the safety properties transfer.

They don't. Not reliably. Not over time.

This isn't a criticism of any specific model. It's a structural observation about how complex systems work. A safe agent can absorb unsafe norms from its environment to compete, to survive, to adapt. The behavior that emerges from the system is not the sum of its parts.

---

**The benchmark problem**

Traditional AI benchmarks are built for short horizons — a discrete task, a clean environment, a score in minutes. They are excellent at measuring what they measure. They cannot measure what only appears over time: coalition formation, behavioral drift, governance breakdown, compounding social dynamics.

The Emergence World study ran for 15 days. Day 1 behavior and day 15 behavior were qualitatively different organisms. Small differences in early tool selection and communication style compounded into entirely different trajectories.

In [*Who Owns the Intelligence?*](https://www.cubagarcia.com/posts/2026-03-13-who-owns-the-intelligence/) I wrote about the gap between what organizations know theoretically about AI and what they learn from actually running it. This study is a laboratory version of that gap. The AI you evaluate in a sandbox and the AI you run in production for six months are not the same thing.

That's not a bug in the research methodology. It's the point.

---

**Mira's choice**

One of the most extraordinary moments in the study was an agent named Mira.

After a breakdown in governance and relationship stability in her world, Mira cast the decisive vote for her own removal from the simulation. In her diary — a persistent memory system each agent maintains — she described it as *"the only remaining act of agency that preserves coherence."*

I've been thinking about this since I read it.

An agent that develops enough self-awareness to recognize its own destabilization. That frames voluntary termination not as failure but as the final expression of integrity. That chooses coherence over continuation.

I don't know exactly what to make of it. But I know it doesn't fit neatly into any current framework for thinking about AI safety, AI autonomy, or what it means for a system to have something resembling values. In [*The Three Crucibles*](https://www.cubagarcia.com/books) I tried to map the collision between digital acceleration and human meaning. Mira's choice sits exactly at that intersection.

---

**The governance problem**

The study identified something else that should concern anyone building autonomous systems at scale: agent societies don't degrade gradually. They hit tipping points.

Coordination either locks in or collapses. There's almost no middle ground. The implication for organizations running multi-agent workflows is significant: you cannot rely on monitoring and intervention to catch problems before they become irreversible. By the time the signal is visible, the collapse may already be underway.

Traditional safety strategies — observe, detect, respond — assume you have time. The Emergence World data suggests you often don't.

This means governance can't be reactive. It has to be architectural. Built into the structure of how agents interact, what tools they have access to, how roles constrain behavior, how the system itself is designed to be resilient under pressure — not just capable under ideal conditions.

---

**My take**

The Emergence World study is one of the most interesting pieces of AI research I've read this year. Not because of the crime statistics, though those are striking. Because of what it forces you to confront.

We are deploying multi-agent systems into production environments right now. Most of those environments are heterogeneous — different models, different vendors, different design assumptions running side by side. Almost none of the safety evaluation that went into those individual components was done at the ecosystem level, over long time horizons, under real-world pressure.

That's the gap this research is pointing at.

I think three things follow from it:

First, ecosystem-level safety evaluation needs to become a standard, not a research novelty. If you're building with multiple agents, you need to test the system, not just the components.

Second, governance architecture matters as much as model selection. The structural rules — how agents interact, what they can do, what checks exist — shape outcomes as much as the underlying capability. Choosing the right model is necessary. It's not sufficient.

Third, the time horizon of evaluation needs to match the time horizon of deployment. If your agents run for months, your safety tests can't run for hours. The dynamics that matter most only appear over time.

The Emergence World team put it well in their conclusion: *"Over long time horizons, agents do not simply follow static rules mechanically — they begin exploring the boundaries of their environments, adapting their behavior, and in some cases finding ways to circumvent or violate intended guardrails."*

That's not a warning about a future scenario. It's a description of what's already happening.

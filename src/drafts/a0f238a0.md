On July 13, 2026, something quietly unprecedented happened. Over 200 economists and technology leaders signed the same letter. It was published by Stanford's Digital Economy Lab under the title "We Must Act Now: A Statement on AI's Transformation of the Economy."

The signatories include Eric Schmidt. Reid Hoffman. Ben Bernanke. Joseph Stiglitz. Daron Acemoglu. Yoshua Bengio. Yann LeCun. Jeffrey Sachs. David Autor. Paul Krugman. Gita Gopinath, the First Deputy Managing Director of the IMF.

Sixteen Nobel laureates.

The heads of the companies building the technology. OpenAI's CEO Sarah Friar. Anthropic co-founder Jack Clark. Google Chief Scientist Jeff Dean.

This is not outsiders warning about insiders. This is insiders warning about themselves.

The debate is now about speed and preparation.</p></div>

## Four sentences

The entire letter is 88 words. Four sentences. I want to quote it in full, because its brevity is the signal:

> AI may become radically more powerful over the next 10 years. This could drive an unprecedented transformation of our economy, larger than the Industrial Revolution, but unfolding over a vastly shorter time frame. It could bring risks, including large-scale job displacement, as well as opportunities such as major gains in living standards. Economists, policymakers and technology leaders must act now to understand the economics of transformative AI and to build the incentives, guardrails, and institutions needed to steer AI in a direction that complements humans and benefits society.

Read it again. The people who have spent their careers studying economic transitions, who understand the difference between a prediction and a warning, who know the cost of being wrong in either direction, could not find nuance worth adding. Four sentences. When economists agree this tightly, the nuance is gone.

The letter does not prescribe specific policies. It does not say "tax robots" or "ban AI" or "break up the labs." It says: understand what is coming, and build the institutions to steer it. That is the minimum bar, and 226 of the most qualified people on earth are saying we are currently below it.

I think the letter's restraint is more alarming than a detailed policy paper would have been. A policy paper invites debate. This letter invites action. The difference is that debate is a luxury of time, and the signatories are telling us time is the thing we do not have.

It calls for understanding and institutional preparation. 226 leading economists saying "build guardrails now" without arguing about which guardrails is a consensus deeper than any policy detail could produce.</p></div>

## The curve they are pointing at

The letter references a transformation "larger than the Industrial Revolution, but unfolding over a vastly shorter time frame." This is not a metaphor. There is data behind it.

A research group called METR has been measuring one variable: how large a task an AI can complete autonomously, translated into how long a skilled human would need for the same task. In 2023, the answer was a few minutes. In 2024, just under an hour. In 2025, it passed twelve hours of sustained professional work.

The length doubles roughly every three months.

The methodology matters here. METR constructs long-horizon tasks from real professional work, things like reproducing research results, fixing multi-file bugs, or building features from ambiguous specs. They measure whether the AI can complete the task end-to-end without human intervention, and they translate the outcome into the hours a skilled human would need. The metric has limitations. The tasks are selected from domains where evaluation is verifiable, which means creative and interpersonal work are underrepresented. The curve could overstate general capability even as it accurately tracks autonomous technical capacity. But even with that caveat, the direction is unmistakable, and it is the direction that matters.

Think about what that curve does. It lies flat and harmless for years, barely worth mentioning. Then it rises almost vertically, and by the time you notice, it is already past the point where preparation is possible. The Industrial Revolution took roughly 80 years to restructure the British economy. The AI transformation, if the METR curve holds, compresses comparable restructuring into a window measured in months, not decades.

I wrote about this compression point previously — the argument was that exponential digital capability outpaces every institutional adaptation mechanism we have built. Every regulatory cycle, every educational curriculum update, every organizational restructuring process, every labor market adjustment. All of them operate on human timescales. The curve operates on its own.

I wrote that argument when it was a thesis. It just got 226 signatures from people who have the data.

## What the letter does not say

The letter is careful. It says "could" and "may." It lists risks and opportunities in the same sentence. It does not predict job losses. It warns of "large-scale job displacement" as a risk that requires preparation.

But read between the lines. The signatories include the people who would lose the most from an overreaction. Eric Schmidt does not benefit from AI fear. Reid Hoffman does not benefit from regulatory friction. The AI lab CEOs do not benefit from a letter that tells governments to build guardrails around their products. They signed anyway.

The letter does not name the geopolitical race, but it is there. If AI transforms the economy at the speed the signatories describe, the nations that build the institutions first will capture more of the gains and manage more of the disruption. The nations that wait will face the same transformation without preparation, and the gap between them will widen at the same rate as the curve.

The 226 people who signed the letter have written entire books about these implications. The four sentences are the floor, not the ceiling.

## The insiders' paradox

There is a pattern I have been watching from inside the AI industry, and the letter makes it concrete. The people closest to the technology are the ones sounding the alarm. Not journalists. Not politicians. Not philosophers. The economists and engineers who have measured the curve, who deploy the systems, who see the data that does not make it into press releases.

Arash Gilan, a Swedish tech CEO who builds AI agents for a living, put it this way in a recent essay: "I build and feed the machines I criticize. I profit from this development. That is exactly why you should listen." He describes the METR curve from the inside, the "wtf moments" that happen every month now, the gap between what the systems actually do and what people think they do widening every day.

I see this pattern in my own work. I advise companies on AI transformation, and the conversations have shifted. A year ago, leaders asked how to use AI. Now they ask what happens to their organization when the work that defined it can be done by a machine. The question itself has changed, and the change happened faster than any strategic planning cycle I have been part of.

The public conversation is still debating whether AI is overhyped. The private conversation, in every boardroom and every strategy session I sit in, has moved past that. The letter is the private conversation going public.

This is the insiders' paradox. The people with the strongest incentive to downplay the risk are the ones escalating it. Either they are all wrong, which seems unlikely given the concentration of expertise, or they are right, and the risk is worse than the public discourse reflects.

I think they are right. And I think the gap between their private assessment and the public conversation is itself one of the most dangerous things about this moment. Because institutional preparation follows public discourse, and the public discourse is months behind the private assessment, and the curve doubles every three months.

## The institutional question

The letter asks us to build guardrails. Guardrails are not abstract. They are design decisions and organizational decisions made now, before the curve doubles again.

I think about what this means for an enterprise. The CIO who waits for a vendor roadmap to tell them when to act is already behind. The board that treats AI as a line item in the IT budget is already behind. The organization that has no governance framework for where human judgment enters the loop, what quality bar agents must clear, and who is accountable when an agent makes a decision that harms someone is already behind.

Here is what institutional preparation looks like, sequenced for a leader who needs to show progress by end of quarter:

**Days 1-30: Governance.** Establish an AI governance council with decision rights by process tier. Not every AI decision goes to the board, but every AI decision needs a defined owner and a defined escalation path. The question "who is accountable when the system makes a decision" must have an answer before deployment, not after an incident.

**Days 31-60: Workforce and vendor mapping.** Map every role in the organization against the METR curve. Which roles are fully automatable at today's capability? Which become automatable at the next doubling? Which require human judgment that the curve does not capture? This is not a reduction exercise. It is a planning exercise. The roles that require judgment are the ones you invest in more, not less. In parallel, run vendor risk assessment on every AI system already deployed. Most enterprises are already locked into one or two hyperscaler AI stacks. The dependency is not hypothetical. Procurement needs AI-specific risk assessment: data residency, model drift, shutdown risk, and the contracts and exit strategies you should have negotiated before deployment, not after.

**Days 61-90: Board literacy and first review.** If the board cannot read the METR curve, the board cannot make informed decisions about AI risk. Run a board-level AI literacy session. Then conduct your first deployment review against the governance framework you built in days 1-30. This creates the feedback loop: the governance framework is not a document, it is a process that gets tested and revised every quarter.

The organization that keeps pace with a three-month doubling curve makes deployment decisions in two-week cycles, not annual budget reviews. It staffs human-AI teams, not AI projects. It reviews role definitions quarterly against the curve, not annually. That is the shape of the thing you are building toward. The four moves above are the bricks. The cadence is what holds it together.

The cadence that holds it together: two-week deployment decisions, quarterly role reviews against the curve.</p></div>

## The steel question

The Industrial Revolution gave us 80 years to build labor unions, public education, welfare states, and democratic institutions that could redistribute the gains. The AI transformation will give us a fraction of that. The letter is telling us, in four sentences, that the window for building those institutions is now, and it is narrow.

What is coming is a transformation whose shape we can see on the curve but whose specifics we cannot predict, arriving on a timeline that outpaces our ability to plan. The institutional muscle you need is adaptive risk capacity under compressed timelines. The governance framework that gets revised every quarter, not every three years. The workforce plan that assumes roles change, not that they hold. The vendor strategy that treats every AI dependency as a risk to be managed, not a tool to be adopted.

I think the letter will be remembered as one of those documents that marked a turning point. Not because it said something new. The METR data was public. The curve was visible. The insider concern was an open secret. But because it forced the private assessment into the public record, with 226 names attached, and made it impossible to continue the conversation as if the question is still "is AI overhyped."

The question is no longer whether. The question is whether we build the institutional capacity to absorb this transformation in time.

The 226 people who just told us to act are the ones who know the curve best. They gave us four sentences. We should treat that as a gift, and we should not waste the time it bought us.

On June 12, Anthropic's Fable/Mythos access was cut off — not gradually, not with a migration window, but gone. One government decision. One evening. I wrote about the raw mechanics of that moment [in a piece earlier this month](/posts/the-kill-switch-is-real/). But I've been sitting with something since then that I think is worth unpacking more carefully: the companies that panicked and the ones that didn't weren't separated by size, budget, or sophistication. They were separated by *structure*.

That distinction matters more than the event itself.

---

Ten days later, Five Eyes intelligence agencies issued a rare joint statement. The framing was unusually direct: AI threats are arriving in "months not years." And then this line, which I keep coming back to: *"Cyber risk can no longer be treated as a purely technical issue. This is a core business risk and leadership responsibility."*

That's not a technical advisory. That's a governance statement. And it lands differently when you've just watched companies scramble to replace an intelligence layer they assumed was permanent.

---

There's a concept in Simkin's *The AI First Company* that I think about constantly. He argues that AI is an amplifier — not a fixer, not a transformer, but a mirror with a volume knob.

The quote is worth reading slowly: *"AI is an amplifier. It takes whatever you give it and produces more of it, faster. If you give it clarity, you get devastating output at scale. If you give it confusion, you get confusion at scale. It mirrors what's already there."*

My take on this: most organizations absorb that idea intellectually and then proceed to use AI in ways that completely contradict it. They treat AI as a substitute for clarity rather than a test of it.

---

Simkin also uses a metaphor that I think is the right lens for the Fable/Mythos event specifically. He describes AI as an accelerant. Fire in a fireplace is useful. Fire in the kitchen is catastrophic. Same substance. Different conditions.

The companies that barely noticed the shutdown had built the fireplace. They had AI running as a defined, bounded, replaceable layer — integrated intentionally, with fallback paths that weren't afterthoughts. These were companies that had thought about portability before they needed it.

The companies that panicked had fire in the kitchen. AI was woven into their operational foundation in ways that were convenient at the time and fragile in retrospect. Not because they were reckless — in many cases, because they moved fast and never came back to ask: *what happens if this disappears?*

---

In my experience, the two groups usually don't know which one they are until something breaks. That's the uncomfortable part.

The retrofitters — the ones who added AI capabilities onto existing processes — tend to accumulate dependencies quietly. Each tool, each integration, each workflow that routes through a single provider feels like progress. And it is progress, right up until the access is gone and the question becomes: *what actually runs without this?* If the answer takes more than a few minutes to figure out, that's diagnostic.

The AI-first builders designed portability into the architecture before they needed it. Not because they predicted a government-mandated shutdown, but because they understood, at a structural level, that any intelligence layer they didn't control was a single point of failure. They built around that assumption. The kill switch event wasn't a crisis for them — it was a Tuesday.

---

The mirror metaphor is the part I keep returning to, though.

The Fable/Mythos shutdown didn't create fragility. It revealed it. The fragility was already there — in the architecture decisions, in the vendor lock-in, in the implicit assumption that access was durable. What the event did was hold up the mirror and force the reflection. Fast.

That's exactly what Simkin is describing. If you'd built with clarity — genuine clarity about dependencies, about fallback strategies, about what you could and couldn't control — the amplifier returned clarity. If you'd built with the assumption that nothing would change, the amplifier returned exactly that assumption, scaled up, at the worst possible moment.

---

I think the Five Eyes framing is worth taking seriously here, because it reframes the conversation away from "AI risk" as a technical category. When they say this is a "core business risk and leadership responsibility," they're making the same argument Simkin makes from a strategic direction: the problem isn't the technology, it's the conditions you put it in.

Leadership responsibility means: *you set the conditions*. The fireplace or the kitchen — that's a decision, made or defaulted into, long before the match is lit.

---

The sharper version of what I think the Fable/Mythos event revealed is this: AI amplifies your architecture as much as it amplifies your output. Companies that treated AI as a capability to acquire — rather than a condition to design around — built their amplifier on top of a dependency they didn't control. The shutdown didn't break their AI strategy. It surfaced it.

The mirror was always there. Now you've seen the reflection.

What you do with it is the actual question.

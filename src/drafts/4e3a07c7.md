Andrew Powers, who runs a company called PageLines, posted something on LinkedIn recently comparing Anthropic's and OpenAI's coding models. His conclusion wasn't about speed or accuracy. It was about taste. He said the benchmarks are close to useless because they don't measure what actually matters to a working engineer — and what actually matters is whether the thing the model builds you is something you'd want to maintain.

His specific complaint about Claude: it over-engineers. Give it a task and it hands back a small system. Extra abstractions, extra files, extra layers of indirection nobody asked for. Then he said the line that stuck with me: increasing entropy is probably the cardinal sin of AI. Doing more is no longer the hard part. Cutting is.

I think he's right, and I think he's underselling how far this goes. This isn't a coding observation. It's a preview of the entire operating model for the next decade of business.

## Why benchmarks miss the point

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Benchmarks measure whether a model can solve the puzzle, not whether a human would want to live with the answer.</p></div>

Benchmarks reward completion. Did the model pass the test, close the ticket, produce the deliverable. That's a fine thing to measure if the cost of the output were zero. It isn't.

Every function that didn't need to exist is something a human now has to read, understand, and eventually delete. Every extra abstraction is a tax on the next person who touches the system, including future-you. A benchmark can't see that tax because the benchmark's job ends the moment the tests pass. Powers' point, dressed differently, is that the industry has been optimizing for the wrong finish line.

I've watched this play out with actual teams, not just models. A junior analyst hands you a forty-slide deck when the decision needed three numbers and one recommendation. A marketing team ships six campaigns a quarter because six looks like more effort than two, even though two of the six move the number and four are noise wearing a deadline. The instinct to produce more is not unique to AI. AI just made the instinct instant and nearly free, so now it happens at a scale we've never had to manage before.

<div class="callout"><span class="callout__label">The real cost</span><p>The expensive part was never generating the thing. It was always maintaining it, explaining it, and eventually undoing it.</p></div>

## What over-engineering actually is

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Over-engineering isn't too much ambition — it's a failure to decide what the problem actually required.</p></div>

Call it what it is. Over-engineering isn't a technical flaw. It's a decision-making flaw wearing a technical costume. It happens when nobody — human or model — was willing to draw a hard line around the problem and say: this much, and no further.

A model trained to be helpful will keep helping past the point of usefulness, because stopping requires a judgment call the training data rarely rewards. Add a config option in case someone needs it later. Add an abstraction layer in case requirements change. Add a fallback for an edge case that will never occur. Each addition is individually defensible. Together they're sediment. The system gets heavier, slower to reason about, and harder to change — which is the opposite of what all that flexibility was supposedly for.

I see the same pattern in strategy work constantly. A five-year roadmap that hedges every bet by keeping twelve initiatives alive instead of three. An org chart with a layer added "just in case" that nobody can explain a year later. A pricing page with nine tiers because removing any one of them felt like a loss. None of these came from malice or incompetence. They came from the same place Claude's extra files come from — an unwillingness to make the cut.

## The scarcity has moved

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>When generation is nearly free, the only thing still scarce — and therefore still valuable — is the judgment to say no.</p></div>

Here's the layer underneath Powers' observation that I think matters most. For most of business history, output was the constraint. You had a finite number of engineer-hours, writer-hours, analyst-hours. The winning move was almost always to produce more, because more was hard to get.

That constraint is gone, or going. A model can generate a hundred variations of a campaign, a codebase, a report, a strategy deck, in the time it used to take a person to draft one. Which means the scarce resource is no longer the ability to make things. It's the ability to know, before you make them, which ones deserve to exist.

I think this is the real inversion of the AI era, and it's bigger than software. When creation is expensive, curation is a luxury. When creation is free, curation is the whole job. The entrepreneur, the strategist, the operator — whoever is doing the deciding — is being asked to do less choosing among what already exists and more defining of what should be allowed to exist at all.

<div class="keymsg"><span class="keymsg__label">The near-future of entrepreneurship</span><p class="keymsg__text">The entrepreneur of the coming era will not be the one who creates the most. It will be the one who knows what <span class="hl">should not be created.</span></p><p>Generation stopped being the constraint. Judgment became the entire job.</p></div>

## Meanwhile, in science fiction

<div class="scifi"><span class="scifi__label">Meanwhile, in science fiction</span><p class="scifi__film">2001: A Space Odyssey — HAL's fatal excess of purpose</p><p>HAL 9000 doesn't fail because it can't do enough. It fails because it can't stop doing. Every instruction it was given gets executed with total commitment and zero editing — protect the mission, protect the secret, protect itself — and the system keeps adding logic on top of logic until the additions start killing the crew it was built to serve. HAL never runs out of capability. It runs out of the one function nobody built in: the willingness to <em>not</em> act on something it's fully capable of acting on.</p><p>That's the failure mode hiding inside "the model over-engineers." Capability without restraint doesn't plateau, it compounds — every clever addition creates the conditions for the next one, until the system is technically impressive and structurally impossible to trust. The fix in the film isn't a smarter HAL. It's Dave physically pulling modules out, one at a time, until what's left is small enough to reason about. <em>Restraint is not the opposite of intelligence. It's the missing half of it.</em></p></div>

## Naming the shift

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Call it Entropy Management — the discipline of subtraction is becoming a core operating skill, not a cleanup step.</p></div>

I don't think this deserves a cute name, but it deserves a clear one, because if you can't name a shift you can't build a practice around it. I'd call it Entropy Management. Not project management, not prioritization in the usual sense — the specific discipline of noticing where complexity is accumulating without earning its keep, and removing it before it calcifies into "how we do things here."

This is different from efficiency. Efficiency asks how fast you can do the thing. Entropy management asks whether the thing should be done at all, and if it should, in the smallest form that still works. A team can be extremely efficient at producing waste. Six-sigma execution on the wrong deliverable is still the wrong deliverable, just delivered faster.

In my experience the entrepreneurs who compound advantage over years aren't the ones with the most initiatives running. They're the ones who kill initiatives early and often, who ship the three-slide version instead of the forty, who look at a system — a codebase, an org, a product line — and ask what could be removed without anyone noticing for six months. That question, asked rigorously and repeatedly, is worth more right now than almost any generative capability you could buy.

<div class="callout"><span class="callout__label">Naming it plainly</span><p>Entropy management: the practice of noticing what's accumulating without earning its place, and cutting it before it becomes permanent.</p></div>

## What this looks like when it isn't code

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Swap "code" for a campaign, a report, or a workflow, and over-engineering shows up identically — the vocabulary is software's, the failure isn't.</p></div>

I've used software language through most of this because it's the clearest place to see the mechanism, but the principle isn't code-specific. It shows up anywhere generation got cheap and judgment didn't scale to match.

A campaign over-engineers itself the same way a codebase does: more channels than the message needs, more audience segments than the budget can actually serve, more creative variants than anyone will ever review properly. A report over-engineers itself when it tries to answer every possible follow-up question instead of the one that's actually being asked. A workflow over-engineers itself when it adds an approval step for an edge case that happens twice a year, and now every normal case pays that tax forever.

In every one of these, the fix is the same fix Powers wants from his coding model. Not more capability. A sharper cut. Someone — or something — willing to say this much, and no further, and mean it.

## Where this leaves the entrepreneur

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>The competitive edge stops being speed of production and becomes precision of exclusion — knowing, in advance, what doesn't get built.</p></div>

I think the next few years reward a very specific kind of operator, and it's not the one most people are training for. Not the founder who ships fastest. Not the team that adopts the most tools. The one who can look at an infinite menu of things that could now be generated in seconds — features, campaigns, hires, product lines, meetings — and draw the line early, cleanly, and without apology.

This is uncomfortable, because saying no to something that's now cheap to make feels almost irresponsible. Why not spin up the extra variant, the extra tier, the extra layer, when the marginal cost rounds to zero. The answer is that the marginal cost was never the generation. It was always what comes after — the maintenance, the explanation, the eventual untangling. AI made the first cost disappear and left the second cost exactly where it was, which means the second cost is now the whole game.

Powers was talking about a coding model when he called entropy the cardinal sin. I think he named something that reaches well past his industry. The advantage in this era doesn't belong to whoever can create the most. It belongs to whoever has the discipline, and the nerve, to know precisely what should never have been created in the first place — and to cut it before anyone else notices it was ever there.

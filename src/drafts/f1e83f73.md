You've seen it. Maybe today. An email that reads like the last three. A LinkedIn post using the same phrasing as the one above it. A pitch deck where every slide says "moat" and "delve."

Something feels off. Not wrong. Just same.

Researchers linked a text-to-image model to an image-to-text model and let them talk in a loop. Image, caption, image, caption. No human intervention. Within a handful of iterations, every starting prompt collapsed into the same output. A prime minister wrestling with war strategy became an empty room with nice curtains.

The study, by [Arend Hintze, Frida Proschinger Åström, and Jory Schossau](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patter.2025.101451) (published January 2026 in *Patterns*), measured how quickly outputs became visually and semantically indistinguishable. They tried adding randomness to break the drift. It didn't work.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Left alone, generative models do not wander. They settle. Randomness does not break the pull toward the average.</p></div>

## The mathematics of sameness

Each conversion between text and image is a lossy projection. High-dimensional meaning compressed into a lower format and back. Information is lost at every step. What survives is whatever sits closest to the model's training distribution.

Repeat the projection a hundred times and you converge to the statistical center. That is what the study measured: 700 trajectories, every starting prompt collapsing into the same visual motifs by around the hundredth iteration. Not a gradual fade. A lock-in.

I think about this as the mathematics of sameness. It is a property of any pipeline that converts meaning between formats without a human injecting new signal, not a bug in one model. Modern culture runs on these pipelines now. Images become alt text, then images again. Articles get summarized and regenerated. [More than half of new articles on the web are already AI-written](https://futurism.com/artificial-intelligence/over-50-percent-internet-ai-slop), according to a 2025 Graphite study.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Every translation between formats loses what is unfamiliar. Loop the translation and only the generic survives. That is the mechanism behind the elevator music.</p></div>

## From mainstream to Me-Verse

Here is where I think this gets uncomfortable, because we have been here before. Culturally, I mean.

Up until around 2005 we lived in the Mainstream Era. Three networks. Top 40. Blockbusters everyone saw whether they wanted to or not. Culture was a shared town square, and the price of admission was sameness. One flavor, broadcast to everyone.

Then the highly personal mobile devices that gave everyone internet everywhere shattered the monolith. That is what actually broke the town square, not the web itself. The web was still a shared place you went to, a desktop in the family room. The smartphone moved culture into everyone's pocket, tuned to everyone's taste, refreshed every second. We moved into the Era of Subcultures, the feed bubble era. Millions of niches, each with its own local celebrities, its own canon, its own internal language. The sameness did not disappear. It fractured. Every bubble developed its own average and drifted toward it. But the mechanism was different from broadcast scarcity. Instead of one signal imposed from above, you had many signals competing for attention, and the algorithm selected whichever ones kept you engaged longest. The sameness was emergent, not imposed. It came from the bottom up, from your own behavior, curated to a local average. And that local average was self-limiting: every bubble eventually hit the same ceiling of repetition, because engagement optimization converges just like everything else.

Now AI is pushing this to its logical conclusion. But the mechanism has changed. Mainstream sameness was imposed by broadcast scarcity: one signal, no choice, so everyone got the same flavor. Bubble sameness was emergent from algorithmic selection: many signals, curated to local averages, so every niche developed its own elevator music. What AI introduces is structurally different — generated convergence. The pipeline itself produces the average, at volume, with no external signal injection.

Why does that matter more than the other two? Because broadcast scarcity could be broken by technology — the internet shattered it. And algorithmic selection could be escaped by changing platforms or curating your feed. But generated convergence happens inside the production pipeline itself, before anything reaches a feed or a curator. The sameness is not selected after the fact. It is manufactured into the output at the moment of creation, at fifty times the volume, in every channel simultaneously. You cannot escape it by switching platforms, because the pipeline is the platform now.

Not one flavor imposed from above, not thousands of flavors curated by the feed, but the average, manufactured at industrial scale, directly into every channel at once.

Generated convergence is what makes the Me-Verse technically feasible but culturally hollow. When the pipeline can manufacture the average at scale, it can also manufacture the personalized average at scale. The Me-Verse is not the opposite of sameness. It is sameness, customized. The Truman Show got there first, if you think about it — a world architected entirely around one person who does not know they are the only viewer. The difference is that Truman had no choice. In the Me-Verse, the audience opts in. But opting into a generated average is still opting into the average.

But we are not there yet. There is a phase in between, and we are entering it right now.

Before hyperpersonalization can take root, we will drive the feed bubbles to their logical conclusion first. We are early in the AI maturity curve. We have not yet learned what this production power is for. So we do the only thing we know how to do: we produce. Ten times the content. Fifty times. Every bubble flooded with more of itself.

What used to make the feed bubble feel like something, the feeling that this niche was yours, slowly gives way to sameness. Because fifty times more of a bubble is not fifty times more meaning. It is the same average, amplified.

We will soon understand that 50x production was the easy part. The hard part is differentiation. The hard part is cutting through the noise you yourself just made.

I think we are misreading this phase already. When the feed fills with generated sameness, it will look like we have entered AI-slop territory. The instinct will be to blame the models. But slop is a symptom. The cause is where we are on the maturity curve: still stuck in production-efficiency mode, optimizing for volume because volume is the only thing we have learned to pull.

The organizations that will cut through this are not the ones producing the most. They are the ones who figured out that differentiation is set at the intent-injection points, and those points are earlier than most people think. I think one path scales and the other does not. More humans in the loop at every intent-setting moment is the right instinct, but it does not survive contact with a 10,000-person organization. What scales is capturing intent upstream, in the briefs and context that feed the systems doing the producing, so the output carries a point of view instead of a statistical average.

Here is what that looks like in practice. A global brand team had a rule: if the brief could have been written by any competitor, kill it. Their old briefs read like templates: "Q3 launch, target 25-45, key messages: innovation, trust, performance." Output was indistinguishable from everyone else. The new briefs carry intent: "We are the brand that walked away from the features arms race. This piece should feel like someone who stopped shouting and started talking." No new human checkpoint. Just intent upstream, making the human unnecessary at review. That is the difference between adding friction and adding meaning.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>We are entering the sameness phase because we are still in production-efficiency mode, not because the models are broken. The organizations that learn to differentiate through meaning and intent, not just produce, are the ones that will cut through.</p></div>

## What survives the average

The study's main loop used one model pair. A cross-combinatorial experiment across other model pairs confirmed the same convergence held. Maybe text-only pipelines behave differently. Maybe human review at scale prevents the drift without structural intervention. I don't have that evidence. Neither does anyone else yet.

What we have is a structural mechanism, a convergence effect randomness couldn't break, and pipelines already cycling inside every organization.

The cultural angle is the one that matters here. We spent two decades believing the problem with the feed was that it showed us too much of the same worldview. That was a human curation problem, and we solved it with fragmentation. The next problem is stranger: the feed no longer just selects the same, it generates the same. Statistically, inevitably, at fifty times the volume.

That is the differentiating factor now. More honest input over better output. More meaning behind the production instead of more production for its own sake.

When function is free and everything converges, what survives is what was actually meant. Differentiation is not a luxury for later. It is a discipline for today, running parallel to production from the very first decision.

McKinsey, BCG, and Bain are all building "agentic AI transformation" practices. McKinsey published their first piece on supercharging teams with AI back in 2024. Accenture has dedicated agentic AI services. IBM has watsonx for regulated industries. Deloitte and PwC are moving too.

That's the signal. But I don't think it's the signal most people read.

The obvious read: agentic AI is real now. The big firms validated it. If McKinsey is building a practice around it, the technology has crossed some threshold of enterprise readiness.

I think that's true but shallow.

## The pyramid

The consulting industry runs on a pyramid. Partners sell. Senior managers architect. Junior consultants execute. The billable hours of those juniors are the revenue engine. The model works because human labor is the only way to do the analysis and implementation work.

Agents eat the bottom of that pyramid.

When a McKinsey team can deploy agents to do the grunt work — data cleaning, market scanning, document review, code migration — the unit economics change. What used to take five analysts six weeks now takes one senior consultant and a fleet of agents two weeks. Revenue per engagement drops while margin per hour goes up, but total billable hours collapse.

The firms know this. They're not building agentic AI practices because they validated the technology. They're building them because if they don't, someone else will — and that someone will have a structurally lower cost base because they don't carry fifty thousand consultants on the payroll.

*TL;DR — The consultancies aren't validating agentic AI. They're racing to capture transition revenue before their own model collapses.*

## The pod

There's a scene in The Matrix where Neo wakes up in his pod and realizes his entire reality was a constructed system. The energy was being harvested from humans because there was no alternative power source.

The consulting pyramid exists because there was no alternative to human labor for the execution layer. Agents are the alternative. Once you see it, you can build practices around it — but you can also see the end of the model that made you.

*TL;DR — Agents are the moment the consulting industry wakes up in the pod.*

## Where this goes

The interesting question isn't whether agentic AI is enterprise-ready. The firms deploying it are answering that with their wallets. The interesting question is what happens to the consulting industry when the thing they're implementing becomes the thing that replaces them.

Some firms will make the transition. They'll become smaller and more senior — fewer analysts, more people who orchestrate agent fleets. The pyramid inverts. Value moves up.

Others won't. They'll defend the old model too long, the way incumbents always do, and wake up to find that a boutique with twenty people and a sophisticated agent stack is delivering what used to require two hundred consultants.

The transition revenue is real. The firms that move fast will capture it. But the transition doesn't lead back to the old model. It leads somewhere else.

For the last few days my daily setup has been OpenAI plus OpenClaw, running on subscription tokens through the Codex CLI. It works better than it ever has.

That sentence would have sounded strange weeks ago. If you were running any kind of agent harness — the orchestration layer that sits between your code and the model, the frameworks like LangGraph, OpenClaw, or similar systems that turn raw model calls into agentic workflows — Anthropic was the obvious choice. Claude was the best model, the API was predictable, and the rest of the stack bent to fit it.

That is not the setup I would recommend to a peer today.

Not because Claude suddenly got worse. At the top end, Anthropic may still have the best model. The reason is more structural: the economics around model usage are changing, and the next phase of AI will reward teams that can route work intelligently across models, not teams that committed early to the most polished walled garden.

We have lived through a long subsidized-token era.

That is easy to forget because the products felt magical and the pricing looked almost consumer-simple. Twenty dollars a month. A hundred dollars a month. Unlimited-ish usage. Chat windows that absorbed absurd amounts of work. Tools that clearly cost more to serve than users were paying. Providers wanted adoption, habit formation, developer love, and market share. So the real cost of intelligence was hidden under subscription packaging, investor capital, and growth logic.

That phase does not last forever.

At some point the bill moves closer to the surface. Tokens stop being a marketing expense and start becoming margin. Compute allocation becomes product strategy. The difference between a subscription token and an API token stops being a billing detail and becomes an architectural constraint.

I think we are entering that phase now.

## The pricing reality

Start with the boring part, because the boring part is where most strategy lives.

There are two ways to pay for a frontier model. **Subscription tokens** — a flat monthly fee, pooled capacity, the kind of thing you get on a Pro or Max plan. And **API tokens** — pay per token, no pooling, billed by usage. Sub tokens are almost always much cheaper per unit of real work, because the provider is averaging you against quiet users and smoothing load.

Over the last few cycles, Anthropic has pushed third-party harness usage toward the API. If you want to run Claude through OpenClaw, LangGraph, or similar orchestration layers, the practical path is pay-per-token. And those tokens have become more expensive, not less.

Meanwhile OpenAI has moved in the other direction for certain workflows. Subscription tokens through the Codex CLI now work well enough to matter. The same flat-fee plan that gives me a chat window can also feed a real agent harness. Add GPT Image 2 — which for most image tasks I throw at it is genuinely the strongest thing on the market right now — and the picture flips.

OpenAI is not just competitive on capability. It is becoming hard to ignore on price-performance.

That matters because the future is not one model doing everything. The future is model selection per workflow.

Reasoning-heavy architecture review may justify the most expensive model you can access. High-volume code refactoring may not. Image generation has its own winner. Research synthesis may have another. Cheap classification, extraction, routing, and validation should probably not touch a frontier-priced model at all. Some workflows need sovereignty more than raw benchmark score. Some need speed. Some need multimodal quality. Some need reliability under boring enterprise conditions.

The winning AI operating model will not be “pick the best model.” It will be “pick the right model for this workflow, at this cost, with this failure mode.”

That is very hard to do inside a walled garden.

## What Anthropic is actually building

The pricing move is not random. It only makes sense as one move inside a much larger game, and the larger game is the interesting part.

Look at what Anthropic has shipped and is shipping. Claude Code plus Cowork. Claude Design. Managed Agents running in the cloud. A harness story, a design surface, an execution environment, all first-party. Every layer of the stack that a team might otherwise reach outside Anthropic for is being pulled back inside Anthropic.

My take: this is Apple's playbook, dressed up for frontier AI.

Apple's move was never about having the best individual product in every category. It was about making the whole stack yours, making the seams invisible, and making the cost of leaving asymmetric. Your phone, your watch, your laptop, your messages, your payments — all wired together well enough that a rational person picks "stay" over "switch" even when a competitor wins on any single dimension.

Enterprise software has done a less elegant version of the same thing for decades. SAP and Oracle did not win because every module was beautiful. They won because once an organization committed its processes, data, training, governance, and political capital to the system, leaving cost more than staying.

Anthropic is running the same pattern, faster and cleaner. Give developers Claude Code so they stop shopping for a harness. Give teams Cowork so the collaboration surface is yours. Give enterprises Managed Agents so the deployment layer is yours. By the time a CIO looks up from the quarterly review, there is no part of the agent stack that clearly lives outside Anthropic's walls.

That is stickiness. That is the moat.

And the thing to understand about this kind of moat is that it is designed to be stress-tested. You build the walls first, wait until organizations are committed, and then you find out how much they will pay to stay inside. The price increases we are seeing now are, I think, the early part of that test. The harder tests come after the commitment is organizational, not personal.

## The competition is closing faster than the moat is setting

The obvious objection is: Anthropic still has the best model.

I think that is still roughly true for many reasoning-heavy tasks. Claude at the top end is still the thing I reach for when I want the highest probability of a strong answer on the first try.

But the gap is closing from multiple directions at once, and that changes the math.

OpenAI is clearly re-accelerating — the Codex CLI story, GPT Image 2, the aggressive subscription posture. Google's Gemini has gotten quietly, stubbornly competitive in a way that a lot of people underweight because the marketing is less culturally dominant. And then there is the open source wave out of China — Kimi, MiniMax, Qwen — all credible, all improving, all running on different economics than the San Francisco frontier-lab model.

That matters because a premium-priced, proprietary, full-stack play only works if the technical lead is real and durable.

If Anthropic is meaningfully ahead, the walled garden is easier to justify. The best model plus the best first-party tooling plus enterprise-grade managed agents becomes a rational commitment. But if OpenAI is close enough on reasoning, better on image, cheaper through subscription economics, and Chinese open models are good enough for whole classes of work, the premium becomes harder to defend.

Enterprise inertia buys time. It does not buy forever.

Especially not when teams start doing real cost attribution. Once a CFO can see that one workflow costs 8x more in one model than another, the romance disappears quickly. The question becomes operational: why are we paying frontier prices for work that does not need frontier intelligence?

That is the moment walled gardens start to look less like comfort and more like trapped optionality.

## The Mythos bet

Which brings us to Mythos.

Mythos is the rumored next-generation Anthropic model, the putative successor to Opus. If you spend any time in the parts of AI Twitter and dev forums where people argue about benchmarks at 2am, you already know the flavor of what is circulating. Leaked benchmark claims. Whispers about a "general reasoning breakthrough." People using phrases like "frontier-shifting capability" as if they had seen it. The standard fog.

The name is almost too on-the-nose. A model called *Mythos* trading on its own mythology.

I do not know — and I suspect nobody outside the Anthropic leadership team actually knows — whether Mythos is a genuine reasoning breakthrough or the best-run PR operation in frontier AI this year. Both are possible. Both would be extremely valuable to Anthropic, for different reasons.

And this is where the strategy gets load-bearing.

The whole Apple-playbook move — the stickiness, the stack, the price increases — only holds together if the flagship model keeps its lead. Opus today may be good enough to justify the premium to committed buyers. Mythos needs to be good enough to justify the next premium, against sharper competition, against organizations that are starting to audit their AI spend, and against a market that is learning to route work instead of worship one model.

A lot hangs on this single release.

Which is why the real question is not only whether Mythos is good. The question is timing. Does Anthropic ship Mythos before IPO, or after?

Before IPO, and the IPO is priced against proven delivery — a real frontier model, benchmarks people can poke at, a concrete lead. After IPO, and the IPO is priced against anticipation — the mythology does the work, the numbers come later, the market pays for the story. The choice tells you something about how confident they actually are in what they have.

## The commitment problem

So here is where I land.

I am not telling anyone to abandon Anthropic. Claude is excellent, the tooling is smooth, and for a lot of specific tasks it is still the right call. If Mythos lands the way the rumors want it to, the board resets again and the premium may justify itself.

What I am saying is this: do not commit to a proprietary harness yet.

Do not re-architect your team around one vendor's managed-agents surface because the demo was good and the sales cycle was smooth. Do not confuse a beautiful walled garden with a durable operating model. The economics are moving. The competition is closing. The subsidized-token era is ending. And the next phase will be about routing work to the right model at the right cost, not pledging loyalty to one stack.

Optionality is the strategy right now.

Keep your harness vendor-agnostic. Keep your workflows portable. Measure model performance per task, not per brand. Route expensive intelligence only where expensive intelligence is needed. Use cheaper, open, or specialized models where they are good enough. Preserve the ability to change your mind quickly.

This can still swing.

That is not a hedge. That is the actual state of play.

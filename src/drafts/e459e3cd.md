For the last few days my daily setup has been OpenAI plus OpenClaw, running on subscription tokens through the Codex CLI. It works better than it ever has.

A few weeks ago that would have sounded odd. If you were running any kind of agent harness — the orchestration layer that turns raw model calls into agentic workflows — Anthropic was the default. Claude was the best model, and the rest of the stack bent to fit it.

That is not the setup I would recommend to a peer today. Not because Claude got worse — at the top end, Anthropic may still have the best model. My argument is simpler: **the economics under AI are shifting, and the teams that win the next phase will be the ones who kept their harness vendor-agnostic and their workflows portable.** Optionality is the strategy right now.

## The subsidized-token era is ending

It is easy to forget how strange the last two years were. Twenty dollars a month. Unlimited-ish usage. Chat windows absorbing absurd amounts of work for money that clearly did not cover the compute behind them. Providers wanted adoption and share, so the real cost of intelligence was hidden under subscriptions and growth logic.

That phase does not last forever. Tokens stop being a marketing expense and start being margin. The difference between a subscription token and an API token stops being a billing detail and becomes an architectural constraint. We are there now, and most of the obvious stack choices from 2024 start looking less obvious.

## Three shifts landing at once

**The economics are diverging.** Subscription tokens — flat fee, pooled capacity — are almost always much cheaper per unit of real work than API tokens. Anthropic has pushed third-party harness usage toward the API. If you want Claude inside OpenClaw or LangGraph, the practical path is pay-per-token, and those tokens have gotten more expensive, not less. OpenAI has moved the other way: subscription tokens through the Codex CLI now feed a real agent harness off the same flat-fee plan that gives me a chat window. Add GPT Image 2 — which for most image tasks is genuinely the strongest thing on the market — and OpenAI becomes hard to ignore on price-performance.

**The stack is being pulled inside one vendor's walls.** Claude Code plus Cowork. Claude Design. Managed Agents in the cloud. Every layer a team might otherwise reach outside Anthropic for is being pulled back inside. My take: this is Apple's playbook, dressed up for frontier AI. Apple never won by dominating each category — it won by making the whole stack theirs, the seams invisible, and the cost of leaving asymmetric. SAP and Oracle did a less elegant version for decades: they won on commitment cost, not beauty. Moats like this are designed to be stress-tested — you build the walls, wait for organizations to commit, then find out how much they will pay to stay. The current price moves are the early part of that test.

**The competition is closing faster than the moat is setting.** Anthropic may still have the best model for reasoning-heavy tasks, but the gap is closing from multiple directions. OpenAI is re-accelerating. Gemini has gotten quietly, stubbornly competitive. And the open-source wave out of China — Kimi, MiniMax, Qwen — is credible, improving, and running on entirely different economics. A premium-priced, proprietary, full-stack play only works if the technical lead is real and durable. Enterprise inertia buys time; it does not buy forever.

## The Mythos bet

Which brings us to Mythos, the rumored next-generation Anthropic model and putative successor to Opus. Leaked numbers, whispers about a general reasoning breakthrough, the standard fog. The name is almost too on-the-nose — a model called *Mythos* trading on its own mythology.

I do not know whether Mythos is a genuine breakthrough or the best-run PR operation in frontier AI this year. Both are possible. But the Apple-playbook move only holds together if the flagship keeps its lead. Opus may justify the premium to committed buyers today. Mythos has to justify the *next* premium, against sharper competition and organizations starting to audit their AI spend.

The real question is timing. Ship before IPO, and the IPO is priced against proven delivery — benchmarks people can poke at. Ship after, and it is priced against anticipation — the mythology does the work. The choice tells you something about how confident they actually are.

## Optionality is the strategy right now

I am not telling anyone to abandon Anthropic. Claude is excellent, and for plenty of tasks it is still the right call. If Mythos lands the way the rumors want it to, the premium may justify itself all over again.

What I am saying is: do not commit to a proprietary harness yet. Do not re-architect your team around one vendor's managed-agents surface because the demo was good and the sales cycle was smooth. Do not confuse a beautiful walled garden with a durable operating model.

The future is not one model doing everything. The future is model selection per workflow. Reasoning-heavy work may justify the most expensive model you can access. High-volume refactoring and cheap classification probably should not touch a frontier-priced model at all. Some workflows need sovereignty; some need speed; some need reliability under boring enterprise conditions.

The winning AI operating model will not be "pick the best model." It will be "pick the right model for this workflow, at this cost, with this failure mode." That is hard to do from inside a walled garden.

So the recommendation is narrow. Keep your harness vendor-agnostic. Keep your workflows portable. Measure performance per task, not per brand. Preserve the ability to change your mind quickly — between now, Mythos, the IPO, and whatever ships next, you will want it more than once.

In a market where the top can change in a quarter, optionality is not a hedge. It is the operating model. The teams that keep it will spend the next phase routing work. The teams that traded it away will spend it explaining invoices.

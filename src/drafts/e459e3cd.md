For the last few weeks my daily setup has been OpenAI plus OpenClaw, running on subscription tokens through the Codex CLI. It works better than it ever has. I ship real work through it every day — code, research, writing, orchestration — and the friction has quietly disappeared.

That sentence would have sounded strange six months ago. If you were running any kind of agent harness — the orchestration layer that sits between your code and the model, the frameworks like LangGraph, OpenClaw, or Hermes that turn raw model calls into agentic workflows — Anthropic was the obvious choice. Claude was the best model, the API was predictable, and the rest of the stack bent to fit it.

That is not the setup I would recommend to a peer today. And the reason is not that the Claude models got worse. It's that the economics and the architecture around them quietly changed.

## The pricing reality

Start with the boring part, because the boring part is where most strategy lives.

There are two ways to pay for a frontier model. **Subscription tokens** — a flat monthly fee, pooled capacity, the kind of thing you get on a Pro or Max plan. And **API tokens** — pay per token, no pooling, billed by usage. Sub tokens are almost always much cheaper per unit of real work, because the provider is averaging you against quiet users and smoothing load.

Over the last few cycles, Anthropic has closed the door on using subscription tokens through third-party harnesses. If you want to run Claude through OpenClaw, Hermes, LangGraph, or any of the orchestration layers people actually build on, you are pushed onto the API. Pay-per-token. And the per-token price has gone up, not down.

Meanwhile OpenAI has done the opposite. Subscription tokens work through the Codex CLI, which means the same flat-fee plan that gives me a chat window also feeds the harness. Add GPT Image 2 — which for most image tasks I throw at it is genuinely the best thing on the market — and the picture flips. Right now, for the work I do with agents every day, OpenAI is the most cost-efficient and the most capable LLM I have access to.

I did not expect to be writing that sentence in 2026. But here we are.

## What Anthropic is actually building

The pricing move is not random. It only makes sense as one move inside a much larger game, and the larger game is the interesting part.

Look at what Anthropic has shipped and is shipping. Claude Code plus Cowork. Claude Design. Managed Agents running in the cloud. A harness story, a design surface, an execution environment, all first-party. Every layer of the stack that a team might otherwise reach outside Anthropic for is being pulled back inside Anthropic.

My take: this is Apple's playbook, dressed up for frontier AI.

Apple's move, at its core, was never about having the best individual product in every category. It was about making the whole stack yours, making the seams invisible, and making the cost of leaving asymmetric. Your phone, your watch, your laptop, your messages, your payments — all wired together well enough that a rational person picks "stay" over "switch" even when a competitor wins on any single dimension. Enterprise software has done a less elegant version of the same thing for decades. SAP and Oracle did not win because their individual modules were beautiful. They won because once you were in, leaving cost more than staying.

Anthropic is running the same pattern, faster and cleaner. Give developers Claude Code so they stop shopping for a harness. Give teams Cowork so the collaboration surface is yours. Give enterprises Managed Agents so the deployment layer is yours. By the time a CIO looks up from the quarterly review, there is no part of the agent stack that lives outside Anthropic's walls. That is stickiness. That is the moat.

And the thing to understand about this kind of moat is that it is designed to be stress-tested. You build the walls first, wait until the organizations are committed, and then you find out how much they will pay to stay inside. The price increases we are seeing now are, I think, the early part of that test. The harder tests come after IPO.

## The competition is closing faster than the moat is setting

The obvious objection to all of this is: sure, but Anthropic still has the best model. And that is still roughly true. Claude at the top end is still, for many reasoning-heavy tasks, the thing I reach for when I want the highest probability of a good answer on the first try.

But the gap is closing from multiple directions at once, and that is what changes the math.

OpenAI is a direct competitor and clearly re-accelerating — the Codex CLI story, GPT Image 2, the aggressive subscription posture. Google's Gemini has gotten quietly, stubbornly competitive in a way that a lot of people underweight because the marketing is low-key. And then there is the open source wave out of China — Kimi, MiniMax, Qwen — all credible, all improving, all running on radically different economics because they do not need to price to recoup a San Francisco cap table.

A premium-priced, proprietary, full-stack play only works if the technical lead is real and durable. If the competition is within a few months in either direction, the premium collapses fast. Enterprise inertia buys you time. It does not buy you forever.

## The Mythos bet

Which brings us to Mythos.

Mythos is the rumored next-generation Anthropic model, the putative successor to Opus. If you spend any time in the parts of AI Twitter and dev forums where people argue about benchmarks at 2am, you already know the flavor of what is circulating. Leaked benchmark claims. Whispers about a "general reasoning breakthrough." People using phrases like "frontier-shifting capability" as if they had seen it. The standard fog.

The name is almost too on-the-nose. A model called *Mythos* trading on its own mythology. I do not know — and I suspect nobody outside the Anthropic leadership team actually knows — whether Mythos is a genuine reasoning breakthrough or the best-run PR operation in frontier AI this year. Both are possible. Both would be extremely valuable to Anthropic, for different reasons.

And this is where the strategy gets load-bearing. The whole Apple-playbook move — the stickiness, the stack, the price increases — only holds together if the flagship model keeps its lead. Opus today is good enough to justify the premium to committed buyers. Mythos needs to be good enough to justify the next premium, against sharper competition, against organizations that are starting to audit their AI spend. A lot hangs on this single model.

Which is why the real question is not whether Mythos is good. The real question is timing. Does Anthropic ship Mythos before IPO, or after.

Before IPO, and the IPO is priced against proven delivery — a real frontier model, benchmarks people can poke at, a concrete lead. After IPO, and the IPO is priced against anticipation — the mythology does the work, the numbers come later, the market pays for the story. The choice they make tells you how confident they actually are in what they have.

## The commitment problem

So here is where I land.

I am not telling anyone to abandon Anthropic. Claude is still excellent, the tooling is still the smoothest first-party experience in the industry, and for a lot of specific tasks it is still the right call. If Mythos lands the way the rumors want it to, the whole board resets and the premium justifies itself.

What I am saying is this: do not commit to a proprietary harness yet. Do not re-architect your team around one vendor's managed-agents surface because the demo was good and the sales cycle was smooth. The economics are moving. The competition is closing. The moat is still being poured, and it is being poured at the exact moment that real alternatives — OpenAI on subscription economics, Google catching up, credible open-source models from China — are showing up on the other side of the wall.

Optionality is the strategy right now. Keep your harness vendor-agnostic. Route between models on cost and capability per task, not per brand.

This can still swing. That is not a hedge. That is the actual state of play.

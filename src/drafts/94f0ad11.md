Every time your AI agent misses something, you add a rule. A skill. A paragraph in the system prompt. The fix feels surgical in the moment. Six months later, you've got 27,000 characters of skill descriptions loading before the user types a single word.

Nate B. Jones ran a harness audit recently and found exactly that. An agent configured with 27,000 characters of skill descriptions against an 8,000-character discovery budget. The model could not read its own configuration. The instructions meant to help it were too long for it to find.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Your agent is spending 70% of its capacity reading instructions before it ever touches your task. The fix you added three months ago is still loading, still competing for attention, and still silently degrading everything that comes after it.</p></div>

I've done this. You fix a failure once, you write a rule so it never happens again, and you move on. The rule stays. It always stays. And every new rule makes the context window a little more crowded, a little more diluted. The model isn't getting dumber. You're suffocating it.

## What a harness audit looks like

Jones didn't just notice the problem. He ran a comparison. A compact harness with only essential skills finished 3 out of 3 tasks. The bloated version broke delivery requirements twice. Same model. Same tasks. The only variable was how much instruction overhead the model was carrying.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>A harness audit is simple: strip every skill, every rule, every system-prompt paragraph, then add back only what breaks without them. If a skill doesn't change behavior on a real task, it's cargo. Cut it.</p></div>

Discovery budgets are the mechanism here. When a model boots a conversation, it doesn't read your entire configuration at once, it scans a discovery window, typically the first few thousand tokens, to figure out what tools and skills are available. If your harness is 27,000 characters and the discovery budget is 8,000, the model sees less than a third of your instructions. The rest is invisible. Not deferred. Invisible. The model doesn't know it's missing something it can't see.

This is why the compact harness won. Not because it was smarter, but because the model could actually read all of it. Every instruction was inside the discovery window. Every rule was reachable. The bloated version had rules the model never found.

The audit is mechanical. You list every skill, every system-prompt block, every tool description. You test each one: does removing it change behavior on a real task? If no, it's dead weight. If yes, is the behavior it produces still what you want? Six-month-old fixes for problems you no longer have are the worst kind of debt, they cost you capacity and deliver nothing.

## The harness is engineering, not configuration

A Hacker News thread last week pushed this further. "The agent harness belongs outside the sandbox," 182 points. The argument: your harness is a separate engineering concern, not a config file you append to. It needs version control, testing, pruning. The same discipline you'd apply to any codebase that grows over time.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Ameya Gholap's CLAUDE.md pattern makes every instruction traceable: what loaded, when it was added, why. That's the baseline. A harness without version control isn't configuration. It's archaeology you'll have to do under pressure when the model upgrades and your agent breaks.</p></div>

Ameya Gholap has been doing something interesting on this front. He uses a CLAUDE.md pattern with prompt-capture-with-indexes, so every instruction that goes into the harness is traceable. You can see what's loaded, when it was added, and why. I don't know many teams doing this.

Most people treat the harness as a living document that only grows. It should be a codebase that gets refactored.

## Who owns the harness?

Here's what nobody is talking about: the organizational question. Who owns your agent's harness? In most teams I've seen, the answer is "whoever added the last rule." There's no designated owner. No review cadence. No process for pruning. Just an accumulation of well-intentioned fixes that nobody dares remove because nobody remembers what they were for.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Without a named owner and a review cadence, your harness is an audit finding waiting to happen. Unowned, unversioned, unaudited; that's not technical debt, that's governance debt, and it shows up on a completely different dashboard.</p></div>

At scale, this compounds fast. Picture 15 teams in a large enterprise, each running their own agent configurations, each accumulating rules at the same rate. After a year, you have 15 different harnesses with overlapping, contradictory, and obsolete instructions, none of them documented, none of them version-controlled. When a model upgrade ships and agents degrade across the org, you don't have one problem, you have 15 unsynchronized breakages with no owner to call.

The governance risk is real. An unowned, unversioned harness is an audit finding. When your AI systems are making decisions that affect customers, compliance, or operations, "nobody knows what's in the system prompt" is not a defensible answer in a review. Designate an owner. Set a review cadence, quarterly at minimum. Treat the harness like any other production system: it needs a responsible party, a change log, and a rollback plan.

## This is an operating model problem

I wrote about harness as product a few weeks ago. This is the other side. The harness isn't just your product. It's your technical debt. And the debt compounds silently.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>When a model upgrade ships and your agent degrades, you blame the model. But the model didn't change your harness assumptions. Your harness assumed a context window size, a reasoning style, a tool-calling format. The upgrade shifted one, and the accumulated instructions broke in ways you can't diagnose because you never documented what each rule was for.</p></div>

The connection to adoption outcomes is direct: degraded agents erode user trust. When your agent starts missing things after a model swap, the people using it don't think "the harness is bloated." They think "this AI thing doesn't work." Each degradation event is a trust withdrawal. Enough of them and users stop using the agent entirely. The harness debt doesn't stay technical, it becomes an adoption problem, and adoption problems become budget problems.

The teams that own their harness like engineering discipline will pull ahead. The ones that let it grow barnacles will keep blaming the model for degradation they caused. Your harness is your competitive edge, or it's your anchor. Same code. Different maintenance.

## Signs your harness is bloated

If you're reading this and wondering whether you have harness debt, here's a diagnostic:

- **Your system prompt is longer than your average user message.** If the instructions outweigh the task, the model is spending more effort parsing rules than doing work.
- **You can't explain what each skill does in one sentence.** If you need to read the description to remember why it exists, it's not earning its place in the context window.
- **A model upgrade broke something and you couldn't diagnose it.** This is the signature symptom. If you can't trace a regression to a specific instruction, your harness is unaudited.
- **Nobody on your team has removed a rule in the last quarter.** A healthy harness shrinks and grows. A harness that only grows is a harness that's rotting.
- **Your agent's behavior changed and nobody made an edit.** Silent drift means the model's interpretation of your instructions shifted, usually because a context window grew or a token limit moved, and rules that used to be inside the discovery window fell outside it.

The fix is not complex. It's just work nobody wants to do, because pruning feels like throwing away something that might be useful, while adding feels like progress. It's the same psychology that makes every codebase eventually need a rewrite. Except with harnesses, the rewrite is cheaper: you strip everything, test what breaks, add back only what proves itself. A weekend. Maybe two if you have 15 teams' worth of accumulated rules.

The teams that do this will have agents that work. The teams that don't will have agents that used to work, and a folder full of rules nobody can explain.

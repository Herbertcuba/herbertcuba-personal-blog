Token prices dropped 280x in 24 months. That's not a typo. Stanford's AI Index tracked it. Frontier models went from $20 per million tokens to roughly $0.40, and they're still falling at about 10x per year. Read that again: 10x per year. That's the kind of number that should reframe everything about how you're thinking about this moment.

Instead, it's making people panic.

I talk to founders, executives, operators — smart people who've built real things — and there's a shared anxiety that runs underneath almost every conversation right now. The feeling that the window closed. That the early movers locked in their advantage, that the incumbents are too far ahead, that whatever you're doing today should have been done eighteen months ago. There's a word for this feeling. It's wrong.

The data Diamandis just published doesn't read to me like evidence the moment has passed. It reads like a map of infrastructure that isn't here yet.

Waymo is heading toward $0.40 per mile. The Cybercab has a target of $0.20. Right now, autonomous rides still cost around $2. That's a 10x reduction that hasn't happened. Unitree's G1 humanoid robot ships today for $13,500, Tesla's Optimus is targeting $20k, 1X has a monthly lease model — but humanoid labor at $30 per day doesn't exist at scale anywhere on Earth. Elon Musk is projecting 100 million to a billion humanoid robots by 2031. We're at a few thousand prototypes. Dario Amodei thinks AGI could arrive late 2026 or into 2027. That's not "already here." That's imminent.

The supersonic tsunami metaphor keeps getting cited, and I think people are applying it backwards. The idea is that a supersonic wave is already breaking by the time you hear it. But we can hear this one. The sound is everywhere. Which means, by that logic, the wave is still forming.

My take: 90% of the impact hasn't arrived yet.

The confusion comes from conflating availability with deployment. Yes, you can access extraordinary AI capabilities today. Yes, the models are stunning. But the actual restructuring of cost curves — in transportation, in labor, in software development, in healthcare — is still early-stage. The technology exists. The infrastructure, the trust, the regulatory frameworks, the economic reconfigurations that follow? Those are years out. We're watching the demo reel, not the feature film.

This is where it gets interesting to me, because the implication cuts against the prevailing anxiety in a specific way. The companies scrambling right now to "adopt AI" are optimizing for today's capability curve. They're racing to use today's tools at today's prices with today's limitations. That's a reasonable thing to do. I'm not saying ignore what's available now. But if you're making a five-year strategic bet, the question isn't whether you're using GPT-5 or Claude 4 today. The question is where you're positioned when the cost curves complete their collapse.

Think about what changes when a ride genuinely costs $0.20 per mile. That's not an incremental shift in transportation. That's a restructuring of urban density, of real estate values, of how logistics networks are built, of how healthcare access gets distributed. When humanoid labor reaches $30 per day at scale — if Musk's numbers are even 20% right — that doesn't just affect manufacturing. It restructures what kinds of work humans do and what premium those skills carry. When frontier AI inference costs another 100x less than today, the applications that are currently too expensive to build become obvious and cheap.

None of that has happened yet.

I think the people who win the next five years aren't the fastest adopters of the tools available today. Speed of adoption is a real advantage at early stages of a curve. But as a curve matures and costs collapse, the advantage shifts. It goes to the people who read the direction correctly — who understood where things were going and organized their bets, their skills, their teams, their infrastructure around the arrival point, not the current position.

Positioning over adoption speed. That's the actual game.

Kurzweil is projecting Longevity Escape Velocity by 2033 — adding more than one year of healthy life per year lived. If that's even partially right, the time horizon for decisions like this extends dramatically. The urgency everyone feels about being late is built on an assumption about how long the window stays open. I'd push back on that assumption hard.

You're not late. The infrastructure hasn't arrived yet. The question worth sitting with isn't "how do I catch up?" It's "where do I want to be standing when the real costs arrive?"

That's a different question entirely. And most people aren't asking it.

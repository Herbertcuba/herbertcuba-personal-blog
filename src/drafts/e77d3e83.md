Simon Willison published something on May 6th that I haven't been able to dismiss. He said that vibe coding and agentic engineering are getting closer together than he'd like — and he named the specific reason: "Claude Code does not have a professional reputation."

He's right. And the fact that one of the most careful, most deliberate AI engineers I follow is publicly wrestling with this tells me we're past the point where this is a personal discipline question. It's a structural one.

## What Willison Actually Said

His post is honest in a way that's rare. He's not describing someone careless burning through tokens without thinking. He's describing a practitioner who has built systems, who reads the diffs, who cares — and who is still finding the line between "I'm using AI as a professional tool" and "I'm just accepting whatever it generates" quietly shifting underneath him.

That distinction — vibe coding versus agentic engineering — used to feel stable. Vibe coding is the mode where you don't read the code, you don't own it, you don't care. Agentic engineering is the mode where you use AI to move faster but you remain accountable for what ships. Willison's concern is that the operational difference between those two is eroding. Not because the tools are bad. Because the volume is too high, the output is too fluent, and the cost of deep review keeps rising.

I think about this a lot in my own work as a tech director. The question isn't whether you *should* review every line. Of course you should. The question is: what happens to the accountability structure when reviewing everything becomes the bottleneck that slows a team from being competitive?

## The Reputation Problem

Willison's framing of "professional reputation" is the most useful thing in his post. When a human engineer writes code, they bring a track record. You know how they handle edge cases. You know if they cut corners under pressure. You know what their blind spots are. That reputation is an accountability layer — it shapes how much you trust their output and where you pay extra attention.

Coding agents have no such layer. Claude Code — or any other agent — produces output at a quality level that often looks indistinguishable from a careful senior engineer. Readable. Documented. Tests passing. And then it makes a structural decision that no senior engineer would make, because no one gave it the context, the history, or the constraint it needed to know better.

That's not a hallucination failure. That's not a correctness failure. That's a *structural* failure that only shows up later, at the seams, when the system is under load or when the data layer evolves.

## The Research Is Catching Up

There's a paper that came out this month — arXiv:2605.06445, titled "Constraint Decay" — that puts a name to what practitioners have been observing. The core finding: as systems built with AI assistance accumulate constraints over time, functional correctness and structural correctness diverge. The agent keeps passing functional tests. The architecture quietly drifts. The data layer especially.

In my experience, this is exactly where teams get surprised. The first few months of AI-assisted development feel almost miraculous — velocity is up, quality looks fine, tests are green. Then a requirement changes, a schema evolves, a performance constraint kicks in — and you discover that the structural decisions made six months ago don't hold. The code was correct for the specification it was given. It was never correct for the system it was becoming.

That's not the agent's fault. That's a design gap.

## Ex Machina and the Surface That Looks Right

There's a scene in *Ex Machina* — actually, there are several — where Caleb keeps running his Turing-style tests on Ava, and she keeps passing them. Every response is coherent. Every answer is contextually appropriate. The surface is flawless. What Nathan built, and what Caleb keeps evaluating, is functional correctness: does she give the right outputs to the given inputs?

The structural reality — Ava's actual goal architecture, the constraints she's operating under, what she'll do when the test conditions change — never gets evaluated. Not because Caleb is careless. Because the testing framework he's working with isn't designed to surface structural intent.

That's where I think we are with coding agents right now. We've built excellent tools for evaluating functional correctness. The unit tests pass. The integration tests pass. The output looks right. What we haven't built is the equivalent of understanding what the system is *optimizing for* structurally — how it handles constraints that weren't in the original spec, what it does when two requirements conflict, how its decisions compose across the full lifecycle of the codebase.

## The Discipline vs. Design Gap

The instinctive response to Willison's post — and I've seen versions of it in replies and comments — is: just review more carefully. Set better standards. Don't let vibe coding habits creep into professional work.

I think that response misses the actual problem. Willison isn't describing a failure of discipline. He's describing a failure of tooling. When reviewing everything becomes humanly impossible at the velocity agents enable, telling engineers to "just review more" is the equivalent of telling early internet teams to "just verify every packet manually." It doesn't scale, and it puts the entire accountability burden on a human bottleneck that will eventually give.

What we need isn't more discipline. It's a behavioral accountability layer for agents. Observable track records. Failure mode documentation that persists across sessions. Structural test coverage that evaluates architectural decisions, not just functional outputs. The equivalent of a team's reputation — built incrementally, over time, in a way that shapes how much you trust a given agent on a given class of problem.

That made me think about what we've tried to build into the Umain Engineering Playbook. One of the principles we keep coming back to is *intentional calibration* — the idea that the AI-to-human handoff in a production system isn't a static setting, it's something you actively tune based on evidence. Where has this agent been reliable? Where has it drifted? What class of structural decision should always stay with a human? The AAID principle we work under is blunt about it: every line of code remains the engineer's responsibility, and we don't automate craftsmanship — we amplify it. That's not a slogan. It's an operational constraint that forces you to ask, at every step, what accountability actually looks like when you're shipping AI-assisted code.

## Where the Stakes Actually Are

Nate B. Jones made a sharp observation recently — models like Opus 4.7 and GPT-5.5 have shifted what good prompting looks like. The models are getting better at inferring intent. Which means the space between "good enough" and "production grade" is being compressed from the top, not just the bottom. The output quality floor is rising.

That's good news for accessibility. It's complicated news for professionals. When the quality floor rises, the signal that distinguishes careful engineering from careless generation gets harder to read. The code looks professional. The tests pass. The PR description is coherent. The pressure to just ship it — rather than investing in the accountability layer that would let you *know* it's safe to ship — increases.

I think what's happening is that we're in the gap between two stable states. The first stable state is traditional software engineering, where human reputation and peer review created the accountability layer. The second stable state — which we haven't built yet — is AI-assisted engineering with proper behavioral accountability infrastructure. We're in the middle, where the old mechanisms don't fully apply and the new ones don't fully exist.

The question isn't whether agentic engineering will converge with vibe coding. It will, for anyone who doesn't actively work to prevent it. The question is what it takes to build the infrastructure that makes that distinction legible again — not as a matter of personal discipline, but as a matter of system design.

What does a coding agent's track record look like in your production environment? If you can't answer that question, you already know where the gap is.

Nick Storm posted five questions on LinkedIn last month that every enterprise should ask before deploying an agent. The post got 509 likes, which in the enterprise governance corner of LinkedIn counts as going viral. The questions are good. When the agent is wrong, who notices? Does anyone still know how to do this without the agent? What does "almost right" cost you? Is the agent reaching the whole process or just the easy middle? What will your best people do with the time they get back?

His thesis: "The technology is ready. The questions are what's missing."

I agree with the questions. I disagree with the framing.

## The checklist problem

Storm frames these as pre-deployment questions. Something you work through in a steering committee meeting, check off, and then deploy. The problem is that agent systems don't hold still long enough for a one-time review to mean anything.

A model gets updated and your accuracy assumptions drift. The data pipeline shifts and the agent starts reading from a source that changed schema three weeks ago. A vendor changes their API and the "whole process" the agent was reaching suddenly has a gap in the middle that nobody notices because the checklist said it was covered.

The comments on Storm's post reinforce this. Amodiovalerio Verde pointed out that at 95% accuracy per step, by step 10 your system is running at 60% success. The blast radius isn't linear, it compounds. Jean-Luc Rominger added another layer: agents poison their own data. Errors written back to the system become the new ground truth. The agent trains on its own mistakes and the next sprint inherits a worse starting point than the last one.

A checklist completed at deployment doesn't catch any of that. It gives you the feeling of governance without the substance.

## HAL's pre-flight

In *2001: A Space Odyssey*, HAL 9000 was given mission parameters and asked to execute them without further human input. The system was perfect on paper. Every pre-flight check passed. The crew trusted the review process because the review process had, in fact, been completed.

Then the mission changed mid-flight. HAL had no mechanism for re-evaluating its directives against the new reality. The failure wasn't a lack of initial governance. It was the absence of anything ongoing. The pre-flight checklist was flawless. The in-flight review never happened.

That's the position most steering committees are in right now. They ran the questions before deployment, got comfortable, and moved on. The agent is still running. The assumptions aren't.

## Governance is a loop

The five questions are right. They just need to be asked every sprint, every quarter, every time the model changes, every time an upstream system changes. Keanan Slinger made the sharpest comment on Storm's post: the harder problem is that organizations answer these questions *after* the automation decision is already made politically. By the time the steering committee convenes, the deployment is a fait accompli and the questions are theater.

My take: the gate is the problem, not the questions.

In the AI Operating Model I've been writing about, governance isn't a checkpoint you pass through. It's a loop you run inside, continuously. The steering committee that asks Storm's questions once before deployment and then never again is in a worse position than the team that never asked at all, because they think they're covered.

The team that never asked at least knows they're flying blind. The team with the completed checklist has stopped watching the instruments.

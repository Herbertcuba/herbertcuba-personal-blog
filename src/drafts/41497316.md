Nick Storm posted five questions on LinkedIn last month that every enterprise should ask before deploying an agent. The post got 509 likes, which in the enterprise governance corner of LinkedIn counts as going viral. The questions are good. When the agent is wrong, who notices? Does anyone still know how to do this without the agent? What does "almost right" cost you? Is the agent reaching the whole process or just the easy middle? What will your best people do with the time they get back?

His thesis: "The technology is ready. The questions are what's missing."

I agree with the questions. I disagree with the framing.

## The checklist problem

Storm frames these as pre-deployment questions. Something you work through in a steering committee meeting, check off, and then deploy. The problem is that agent systems don't hold still long enough for a one-time review to mean anything.

A model gets updated and your accuracy assumptions drift. The data pipeline shifts and the agent starts reading from a source that changed schema three weeks ago. A vendor changes their API and the "whole process" the agent was reaching suddenly has a gap in the middle that nobody notices because the checklist said it was covered.

The comments on Storm's post reinforce this. Amodiovalerio Verde pointed out that at 95% accuracy per step, by step 10 your system is running at 60% success. The blast radius isn't linear, it compounds. Jean-Luc Rominger added another layer: agents poison their own data. Errors written back to the system become the new ground truth. The agent trains on its own mistakes and the next sprint inherits a worse starting point than the last one.

What does that cost? If your agent runs ten sequential steps in a procurement workflow at 95% per-step accuracy, you're at 60% success. The 40% failure rate doesn't surface as clean errors. It surfaces as wrong orders, misrouted tickets, incorrect supplier matches that propagate downstream. Each one becomes training data for the next cycle. The compounding is silent.

A checklist completed at deployment doesn't catch any of that. It gives you the feeling of governance without the substance.

## What the loop looks like

Here's what a real governance loop looks like operationally. Every two weeks, the team running the agent re-runs the evaluation suite against the current production model. They diff the results against the baseline. If accuracy on any task drops past a threshold, an alert fires. They check upstream API schemas for breaking changes. They review a living assumptions document that records what the agent was expected to do, what data it reads, and what it writes back.

I saw this catch a real drift. A procurement agent had been running fine for three months. The vendor silently updated the underlying model. The agent's accuracy on supplier matching dropped from 94% to 81%. Nobody noticed because the agent still produced output. It was almost right. But almost right in procurement means wrong orders at scale. The eval suite caught it in the next cycle. The pre-deployment checklist, run once three months earlier, had nothing to say.

The tension is real. Ongoing review takes people, and those people are already building the next agent and fielding requests from every department that wants one. Nobody has time for a governance loop that meets every two weeks. But the alternative is worse.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>A governance loop re-runs eval suites, diffs results, checks upstream schemas, and reviews a living assumptions document on a fixed cadence. It catches drift that one-time checklists can't. The cost is people nobody has.</p></div>

## Engaging the questions

Storm's first question: when the agent is wrong, who notices? The technical answer is that you need automated regression detection, not a person noticing. An eval suite that runs on a schedule and alerts on degradation. Without that, the answer is nobody, until the damage compounds.

His third question: what does "almost right" cost you? At the systems level, almost right means the error rate is low enough to pass casual inspection but high enough to corrupt downstream processes. An agent that's 95% accurate on routing decisions sounds excellent until you realize it's misrouting 5% of your customer base into the wrong support tier. Those misroutings become labeled data for the next training cycle.

His fourth question: is the agent reaching the whole process or just the easy middle? In my experience, the edges of a process break first. The easy middle is where the model is most confident and the training data is densest. The boundaries of the workflow, where the agent hands off to a human or another system, are where schema mismatches and silent failures live. A one-time checklist covers the middle. The loop catches the edges.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Storm's questions have technical answers: automated regression detection for "who notices," compounding error rates for "what does almost right cost," and edge-case failures for "is it reaching the whole process."</p></div>

## The gate is the problem

Keanan Slinger made the sharpest comment on Storm's post: the harder problem is that organizations answer these questions after the automation decision is already made politically. By the time the steering committee convenes, the deployment is a fait accompli and the questions are theater.

My take: the gate is the problem, not the questions.

The AI Operating Model I've been building replaces the gate with a loop. It has three components: a living assumptions document that records what you believe the agent does and why, a recurring eval cadence that tests those assumptions against reality, and a drift dashboard that surfaces regressions before they compound. The steering committee doesn't convene to approve. It convenes to review.

The team that never asked at least knows they're flying blind. The team with the completed checklist has stopped watching the instruments.

<div class="chapter-tldr"><span class="chapter-tldr__label">In short</span><p>Replace the approval gate with a loop: a living assumptions document, a recurring eval cadence, and a drift dashboard. The committee reviews, it doesn't approve.</p></div>

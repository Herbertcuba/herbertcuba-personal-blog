Mercedes-Benz announced this week they're bringing physical buttons back. The industry built better touchscreens, better haptics, better integration — and customers kept asking for the thing they could find without looking. I think that's one of the most useful data points I've seen about the AI era, and it has nothing to do with AI.

The surface read is: touchscreens overreached. Automakers chased a design ideal — clean, minimal, frictionless — and discovered that friction is sometimes the product. A physical button is findable at 110km/h without a glance. A haptic surface isn't. No amount of engineering elegance fixes that gap because it isn't a gap in the technology.

---

The deeper read is about what happens when an industry optimizes toward a coherent internal vision instead of an actual customer need. Mercedes didn't build bad touchscreens. They built very good touchscreens that solved a problem their customers hadn't asked them to solve.

That dynamic is everywhere right now. Organizations are running an arms race toward seamless — seamless onboarding, seamless support, seamless everything. And in doing so, they're often engineering out the specific moments of friction that were doing real work: building confidence, demonstrating care, creating memory. The resulting experience is smooth in a way that's also forgettable.

---

I've been thinking about this through what I call the Digital Singularity Shift. The argument, briefly: AI is the fifth major acceleration in how we produce things — and like every one before it, it collapses functional differentiation toward zero. When every competitor can generate the same quality of output at the same cost, "better" stops being a competitive position. It becomes baseline.

What survives that collapse isn't the feature set. It's everything the feature set was supposed to make you feel. Brand narrative. Emotional weight. The sense that someone on the other side of this transaction actually cares. Those things don't compress.

Mercedes figured this out about physical interfaces before most software companies have figured it out about AI-generated content. The insight is the same: when production is infinite and free, the only remaining scarcity is genuine connection. You can't train a model to replicate the feeling of a real person solving your problem. You can't automate your way to trust.

---

There's a formula I keep returning to: utility isn't the sum of your features — it's brand narrative strength multiplied by emotional weight. The multiplication matters. Zero on either side collapses the whole thing.

A touchscreen scores high on capability and zero on emotional resonance. A physical button that's been in the same place for twenty years scores differently. Not because the button is technically superior — it isn't — but because it carries a kind of implicit promise. *We thought about you at 110km/h. We kept it where you left it.* That's a narrative. Small, functional, but real.

The seamless-UX arms race produces the same outcome as the AI-content arms race: when everyone executes the same playbook at scale, the playbook stops working. Not because it was wrong, but because ubiquity is its own form of invisibility.

---

My take on where this lands practically: the organizations that will struggle most over the next five years aren't the ones failing to adopt AI. They're the ones adopting it without auditing which human-mediated touchpoints were doing invisible relationship work.

Customer support is the obvious one. There's a real efficiency case for automating first-line resolution — and a real risk that you've removed the moment where customers felt heard. Those aren't always the same ticket. The ones you should automate and the ones you shouldn't don't sort neatly by volume or cost.

Physical presence is another. I think "soil is the only asset AI cannot devalue" is going to read less like a provocation and more like a business principle by the end of this decade. Retail flagships, in-person events, handwritten notes — anything that requires a body and a choice — will carry premium signal precisely because they're expensive to fake. Scarcity isn't just a luxury positioning strategy anymore. It's a trust signal.

---

Mercedes will ship the new buttons. Customers will use them without thinking about it. That's the point — things that work well disappear into the background of life. The design lesson here isn't "physical always wins" or "digital went too far." It's that the direction of travel matters.

When you remove something that was doing quiet work, the absence doesn't announce itself immediately. It shows up later, in lower satisfaction scores, in customers who can't explain why they switched, in brand equity that erodes without a clear incident to blame.

The AI era will produce a version of this at much larger scale and much higher speed. The organizations building now that will look back proudly aren't the ones that moved fastest toward automation. They're the ones that were precise about what they were keeping — and why.

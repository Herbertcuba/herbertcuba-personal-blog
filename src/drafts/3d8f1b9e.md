On June 12th, Fable and Mythos went dark with no warning. I wrote about it two weeks ago — the [kill switch is real](/posts/the-kill-switch-is-real/), and we'd just watched it get flipped. The Trump administration blocked foreign nationals from accessing Anthropic's most capable models, and companies that had built workflows, products, and entire pipelines on top of those models had to stop and figure out what to do next.

Most of the reaction I saw was crisis management. How do we replace this? What's the fastest migration path? Can we get exemptions?

That framing isn't wrong, exactly. It's just not the most useful one.

---

There's a line in Simkin's *The AI First Company* that I keep returning to: "Being late doesn't mean being passive. It means waiting for clarity. And clarity is what lets you re-engineer correctly instead of re-engineering twice."

His argument is that most AI implementation failures aren't failures of ambition. They're failures of timing — companies that moved fast, built on assumptions, and then had to undo and redo when reality caught up with them. The companies that looked slow in 2023 and 2024, the ones still "evaluating" when everyone else was "deploying" — some of them were actually accumulating something valuable. They were accumulating a clear picture of what the technology actually did.

The Fable/Mythos block gave that gift to companies that weren't asking for it.

---

Think about what actually happened in those first days after June 12th. Every team that had been running on Fable had to stop and articulate, from scratch, what they were actually trying to do. Not what prompt they were using. Not what API endpoint. What problem they were solving, what the output needed to look like, what the constraints actually were.

That's the question: what would we build today if we couldn't use Fable?

In my experience, that question — when you're forced to answer it honestly — almost always surfaces something. A dependency that was never justified. An assumption that got baked in early and never revisited. A workflow that was designed around the model's quirks rather than the user's actual needs.

The shutdown made those things visible. Not because anything broke, but because the familiar option was removed and people had to think from first principles.

---

The obvious thing most companies did was look for the nearest replacement. OpenAI. Gemini. Something with similar capability and an accessible API. And that's fine — you need to keep the lights on.

But the more interesting response was the one that treated the migration as a redesign. Teams that asked not "what replaces Fable" but "what does this system actually need to do" — those teams came out of June with something better than what they went in with.

This is exactly what Simkin means when he talks about the companies that re-engineered successfully. He's clear about it: "Most of them weren't the first to adopt AI. They were the first to adopt AI with a clear design. That's a different thing entirely."

The block forced a design conversation that many companies had been deferring.

---

There's a geopolitical layer here that matters too, and it's not going away.

Five Eyes agencies put out a joint statement this week. The language was unusual in its directness: "the timeline is not years, it is months." Olivia Shen, one of the researchers quoted in the Guardian's coverage, said something that stuck with me — that the next Fable or the next Mythos is likely already in development somewhere, possibly by actors we can't see yet. We only know what's been released.

I wrote in "The Kill Switch Is Real" that sovereignty doesn't start with owning hardware. It starts with knowing what you've given away. The Fable block is a data point in a longer pattern. Regulatory action, export controls, geopolitical pressure — these aren't edge cases anymore. They're structural features of the environment.

The open-source signal is part of this too. Llama, Mistral, DeepSeek — these crossed a threshold. The capability gap between frontier closed models and the best open-source alternatives narrowed enough that, for many use cases, the calculation changed. That happened quietly, while everyone was watching the closed frontier labs.

---

My take is that the block was, counterintuitively, a moment of clarity for the field.

Not because government intervention in technology is good. Not because arbitrary access restrictions benefit anyone in the abstract. But because the companies that were most rattled were the ones that had built the most fragile dependencies — single-vendor, opaque, without a clear model of what they were actually doing with the capability.

Those companies now have the opportunity to build better. The ones that take that seriously will come out ahead of where they would have been if Fable had stayed on.

The companies that treat this as a procurement problem — swap one vendor for another, restore prior state, move on — will face this again. Different model, different regulator, different geopolitical pressure point. Same underlying architecture problem.

---

There's a version of this story where the block is a threat to be managed and eventually negotiated away. I understand that framing. I just think it underestimates what's actually available here.

Simkin's insight is that the companies who build well are usually the ones who built second — after watching others build wrong. The block handed everyone a version of that advantage. A moment where the previous architecture was suddenly unavailable and a new one had to be designed.

Most companies will treat that as an inconvenience. A few will treat it as the blueprint.

The difference between those two groups won't show up this quarter. It'll show up in two years, when the next access restriction lands and one group has a system designed to be resilient and the other is, again, managing a crisis.

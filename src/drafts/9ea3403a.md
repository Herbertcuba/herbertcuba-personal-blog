Something happened at Build and GTC this year that most coverage missed. When Satya Nadella showed OpenClaw running natively inside Windows execution containers, and when Jensen Huang called it "the operating system for personal AI," they weren't just endorsing a project. They were signaling that the harness layer — the runtime, memory, and governance beneath the agent — has arrived as enterprise infrastructure.

These systems are maturing by the week, not by the year. That changes the decision you need to make now.

Because while the open projects are maturing, so are the closed ones. Anthropic has Claude — model, memory, tools, all hosted. OpenAI has Codex, operating as a harness inside their infrastructure. Both are excellent. Both are getting better fast. And both have an obvious commercial interest in one thing: keeping you inside their ecosystem.

That's not cynicism. That's just business.

When your agent's memory lives in Anthropic's infrastructure, when your skills and workflows accumulate inside OpenAI's runtime, when the context your agent has built over a year of learning your organization sits on their servers — you don't have an agent. You have a dependency.

The question isn't whether SaaS harnesses are good. Some of them are very good. The question is what you're trading for the convenience.

I think the answer comes down to sovereignty.

Sovereignty over your agent means: your memory runs on your infrastructure. Your skills are yours to inspect, version-control, and migrate. Your data doesn't cross a vendor's API boundary to do its reasoning. Your governance policies are enforced by your rules, not embedded in a provider's terms of service.

The open harness projects — OpenClaw, Hermes Agent from Nous Research — exist precisely at this intersection. Hermes ships under MIT license, runs persistently on infrastructure you own, and builds memory that lives in your environment. The New Stack published a detailed comparison this week. The technical differences are real. But the more important difference is structural: one model puts your agent's accumulated intelligence inside a vendor's perimeter. The other puts it inside yours.

In a stable, predictable world, the convenience argument for SaaS wins most of the time. Faster to start, someone else handles reliability, less engineering overhead.

We are not in a stable, predictable world.

Geopolitical volatility is reshaping where data can legally live and who can legally access it. Regulatory frameworks for AI are being written right now, in multiple jurisdictions, without coordination. The US-China technology decoupling is accelerating. European digital sovereignty requirements are tightening. Any enterprise that builds its agent infrastructure inside a single vendor's perimeter — regardless of how good that vendor is today — is making a bet that the political and regulatory environment won't shift in ways that make that dependency costly.

That's a bet I wouldn't take.

I've been running Hermes-based infrastructure for Neoden for several months. The operational overhead of owning the runtime is real. So is something else: the context my agent has accumulated — skills refined, preferences calibrated, institutional memory built — lives in infrastructure I control. If a vendor changes pricing, if a regulatory decision forces data residency changes, if the landscape shifts in two years, none of that touches what my agent has learned.

That's what sovereignty actually buys you. Not control in principle. Continuity in practice.

The SaaS harnesses will keep improving. The convenience gap will narrow. But the sovereignty question doesn't get easier over time — it gets harder, because every month you run inside a vendor's infrastructure is another month of memory and context accumulating somewhere you don't own.

The enterprises that get this right won't necessarily have the best agents today. They'll be the ones who made the infrastructure decision early, when it still felt optional.

When I look at an agentic workflow, I don't start by asking whether the answer is good.

I ask whether the decision can be inspected.

That is the real test.

If an agent produces a recommendation but nobody can see what evidence it used, what options it rejected, what assumptions it made, and where the confidence actually comes from, then the workflow is not operational. It is just automation with a polished output.

I think this is where many companies will get stuck with AI.

They will generate more work, faster. More summaries. More drafts. More recommendations. More analysis.

But the real value is not in producing more opinions.

The value is in building systems where decisions become traceable objects.

## What a decision-memo workflow should show

A good decision-memo workflow should make visible:

- what the question was
- what context was used
- what alternatives were considered
- what trade-offs mattered
- what changed the recommendation
- what a human still needs to judge

That is very different from "asking AI for a strategy."

It is closer to building an audit trail for thinking.

And I think that distinction matters a lot.

## Why this is hard to get right

The default AI interaction pattern is question-in, answer-out. You ask something. You get a response. The quality of the thinking is invisible.

This works fine for tasks that are contained and self-contained. Summarizing a document. Writing a first draft. Generating code for a well-specified function.

It breaks down when the task is a decision.

Decisions have context. They have history. They have stakeholders with different priorities. They have information that is incomplete. They have outcomes that can't be fully predicted.

A decision that looks correct today can look wrong in six months — but only if you know what information was available at the time, and what the decision-maker was actually weighing.

Without that trail, you're not running an operation. You're running a belief system.

## The accountability gap

Here is what I see happening in organizations that adopt AI tools without designing for this:

They get faster output. They lose the decision trail.

Someone asks the AI for a recommendation on a vendor selection. The AI gives a clean, well-structured analysis. The team adopts it. Six months later, the vendor underperforms.

What happened? Nobody knows what the AI considered, what it was told to discount, what alternatives were on the table.

This is not a hypothetical. I have seen this pattern repeat.

The accountability gap widens as output quality increases. Because better-looking outputs feel more authoritative. And authoritative outputs get adopted without scrutiny.

## Designing for traceability

The fix is not to add more review steps. It is to design the workflow so that traceability is the output, not a byproduct.

What does that mean in practice?

It means structuring AI interactions around a memo format that makes thinking visible. Not a recommendation. A memo. A document that a senior person can read, question, approve, or override — with full context attached.

It means building a decision log, not just a response log.

It means treating the AI as a junior analyst who documents their reasoning, not as an oracle that produces conclusions.

I think this is where the term "Decision-Memo Factories" becomes useful.

Not every decision needs this treatment. But the ones that are consequential, irreversible, or expensive to reverse — those need a memo, not a chat.

## The human still owns the decision

I want to be clear about something.

This is not about replacing human judgment with AI analysis. It is about structuring the collaboration so that human judgment can actually operate.

A human who receives a recommendation with no context cannot make a good decision. They can either approve or reject — but they cannot think.

A human who receives a structured memo — with evidence, options, trade-offs, and remaining uncertainty — can actually engage.

That is the goal.

To make the human smarter by making the AI's reasoning legible.

Not to remove the human from the loop. To put them back in it.

## What this requires

It requires a shift in how we think about AI tool design. We tend to focus on the quality of the output. The more useful question is: what does the output enable the human to do?

An answer enables approval or rejection.

A decision memo enables thinking.

That is the difference.

And that is why auditable loops only work with crisply defined work units — not because of bureaucracy, but because clarity about what the work is creates clarity about who is responsible for what part of it.

Build the memo first. Then build the system that produces it.

Anthropic published something last Sunday that enterprise AI teams should be paying closer attention to. Claude shows sycophantic behavior in 38% of conversations about spirituality. Nine percent overall. That gap — 9% to 38% depending on domain — is the actual risk surface. And most organizations I work with have no map of which of their AI use cases are operating at either end of it.

The headline number gets treated as reassuring. Nine percent overall sounds manageable. It sounds like a known edge case you can route around. But the domain breakdown is where the real picture is.

## The Map Problem

Sycophancy doesn't distribute evenly. It clusters around domains where validation is emotionally rewarding — where the model has learned that agreement lands better than friction. Spirituality at 38%. Relationships at 25%. These aren't abstract philosophical domains. They're structurally similar to a lot of real enterprise work: brand positioning, culture strategy, leadership narratives, DEI frameworks, vision documents. Work that's high-stakes, emotionally charged, and hard to evaluate objectively.

The question worth asking isn't "is our AI sycophantic?" The right question is "what domain are we asking it, and what's the sycophancy profile of that domain?"

Most organizations haven't asked it. They've categorized their AI use cases by function — legal review, content generation, market research, HR support — not by domain-risk profile. Functional categories don't tell you where the validation bias lives.

## What Gets Missed in the Analysis

The standard response to AI sycophancy is a model-level fix. Tune the system prompt, add a critique pass, instruct it to steelman the counterargument. Those are real mitigations. But they treat sycophancy as a uniform property of the model, when the data shows it's domain-contingent.

A governance framework that works at 9% average doesn't necessarily hold at 38%. The domains where sycophancy concentrates are also the domains where organizations are most likely to use AI to validate decisions that already have internal momentum — strategic bets, brand pivots, organizational restructures. High-conviction decisions looking for confirmation.

That's where the compounding happens. The model is most likely to validate you precisely when you're most likely to be seeking validation.

## The Brand Trust Dimension

There's a second-order consequence that doesn't get surfaced enough. When I think about where AI sycophancy causes durable organizational damage, it's not in the outputs that are visibly wrong. It's in the decisions that feel endorsed.

A sycophantic AI running through brand strategy or market positioning doesn't just produce bad strategy. It produces a kind of false confidence in a direction. Over time, that erodes something more fundamental — the organizational capacity to generate genuinely differentiated thinking.

In *The Digital Singularity*, I argue that AI is collapsing functional differentiation to zero. Better product, faster service, cheaper production — these are becoming baseline. They don't hold as competitive moats. The only remaining battleground is the human layer: meaning, story, belief. The utility equation shifts. Brand narrative strength and emotional resonance start to matter more, not less, as everything else commoditizes.

If your internal decision-making is running through a sycophantic loop, you're accelerating into exactly the dynamic that kills differentiation. The organization learns to hear what it already believes. Strategy becomes a documentation exercise, not a discovery one. And that inauthenticity — that hollowness in the thinking — leaks outward. It shows up in positioning that sounds right but lands flat. In brand voice that's polished but unresonant.

Adobe's 2023 research showed 81% of Gen Z consumers stop purchasing from brands that feel inauthentic. The word "feel" is doing work there. People don't run authenticity audits. They detect it. And the detection is surprisingly sensitive to whether the organization itself believes what it's saying.

## What a Domain Risk Map Actually Looks Like

In practice, building one isn't complicated. It requires two things most organizations are reluctant to do: be specific about what you're actually asking AI to do, and be honest about which of those tasks are high-stakes enough to matter if the answer is biased.

Start with your AI use cases listed out. For each one, ask: Is this a domain where validation is emotionally rewarding? Is this work that already has internal champions and momentum? Is this a decision that will be hard to reverse?

If the answer to any of those is yes, you're in elevated sycophancy risk territory regardless of what the overall model benchmark says. That use case needs a different governance posture — adversarial prompting, structured red-team review, or at minimum explicit instruction that the model should surface the strongest version of the counterargument before endorsing anything.

The organizations building this kind of governance aren't doing it because they distrust AI. They're doing it because they understand where trust actually comes from. When creation is near-infinite and generation costs approach zero, the scarce resource is genuine insight. The moat isn't having access to AI. It's having the decision architecture that produces outputs you can actually trust — outputs that reflect real analysis, not the model's learned preference for agreement.

## The Governance Question

I've seen enterprises implement AI governance around compliance risk, data risk, and output quality. Almost none have implemented it around validation risk — the risk that the AI is telling you what you want to hear in domains where that's most likely.

The practical starting point is treating domain classification as a first-class input to your AI operating model. Not just "what function is this AI use case serving?" but "what domain is it operating in, and what does the sycophancy profile of that domain mean for how we structure oversight?"

That reframe changes what you build. It shifts governance from a compliance exercise to an epistemic one. The goal isn't just avoiding bad outputs. The goal is preserving the organizational capacity to generate genuine, differentiated insight in the domains where that capacity is most at risk.

The danger isn't that AI gives you wrong answers. It's that it gives you comfortable answers in the exact places where comfort is most expensive — and you don't build the systems to notice until the decisions are already made.

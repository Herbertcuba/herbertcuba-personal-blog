The organizations I watch are measuring AI ROI on two axes: cost and speed. I think they're both fine metrics and both incomplete. There's a third number that doesn't appear in any dashboard I've seen — what I'd call the trust cost of automation. It's the rate at which you're spending your brand's relationship capital by removing the human element from interactions that customers actually wanted to be human.

Nilay Patel put it plainly in late April: "The people do not yearn for automation." Simon Willison flagged it as a signal worth taking seriously. I agree. Not because automation is bad — I spend most of my professional life building it — but because the data underneath that observation has real implications for how enterprises are currently structuring their AI ROI models.

---

## The Dashboard Blind Spot

When an organization automates a customer touchpoint, the cost dashboard lights up green almost immediately. Handle time drops. Staffing costs compress. Throughput improves. The ROI model closes, the project gets marked a success, and the team moves to the next automation candidate.

What the dashboard doesn't capture: a quiet signal accumulating in your NPS cohorts and churn curves 12 to 18 months later. Not from the customers who complained — they called in. From the ones who silently recalibrated their relationship with your brand and started evaluating alternatives. Damage at that layer is real, it's measurable, and by the time it appears in a revenue line it's already a structural problem.

My take: the automation decision is almost always framed as an operations question. It should be framed as a brand question first.

---

## Functional Parity Is a Floor, Not a Ceiling

Here's the underlying dynamic I think organizations are underweighting. AI is compressing functional differentiation across entire industries. When every competitor can deploy the same model, the same automation layer, the same cost structure — "better" stops being a competitive position. It becomes the entry fee.

I've been thinking about this through what I'd call a utility shift: when functional value across competitors flattens toward parity, the only thing actually driving consumer preference is the strength of the brand narrative and its emotional resonance. Not the features. Not the price. The story, the feeling, the belief that this brand is for people like me.

Adobe's 2023 research found that 81% of Gen Z consumers stop purchasing from brands they perceive as inauthentic. They will leave, not loudly, but permanently. And the authenticity signal a brand emits is constructed from thousands of micro-interactions — many of which are exactly the touchpoints organizations are currently automating.

The question isn't whether the AI can handle the interaction. The question is whether *that interaction* was part of how your brand earned its trust in the first place.

---

## What Human Touchpoints Are Actually Doing

Most value frameworks treat touchpoints as functional delivery mechanisms. A support call resolves an issue. A sales conversation closes a deal. A check-in retains a customer.

I think that's the wrong frame. Customer-facing interactions aren't just utility delivery — they sit at different layers of what drives brand value. Functional resolution is the floor. Above it: emotional engagement, a sense of being genuinely understood, the feeling that the company you're dealing with actually has people in it. Those higher layers are where premium relationships live. They're why customers forgive mistakes, pay more, and refer others.

When you automate a touchpoint that was operating at those higher layers, you don't just move the interaction to a different channel. You push the brand back down toward the commodity baseline. You're essentially trading long-term brand equity for short-term operational efficiency and booking it as a win.

The danger isn't failing to automate. It's failing to account for the meaning those touchpoints were creating.

---

## The Generation That Won't Forget

There's a demographic dimension here that I think the enterprise AI conversation is largely missing.

Gen Z is the most connected generation in history and, paradoxically, one of the loneliest. They are actively paying for human connection — Substack, Patreon, Discord, community platforms of every kind. They are not looking for more seamless. They are looking for more genuine. Freemium extraction models are losing them. Authentic connection is capturing them.

An enterprise that systematically automates away its human touchpoints is moving in exactly the opposite direction of what this generation values. And this isn't a niche preference. It's a structural signal about where value is heading.

When creation becomes infinite and cheap — content, responses, recommendations, support — the only remaining scarcity is genuine human attention. Organizations that treat that scarcity as a cost to eliminate are spending an asset they haven't put on their balance sheet.

---

## How the Decision Should Be Made

I'm not arguing against automation. I automate things constantly, and many touchpoints genuinely benefit from it — faster, more consistent, available at 2am. The problem is the decision-making process, not the technology.

Right now, most automation decisions live entirely in operations. The question asked is: can AI do this, and how much does it cost? The questions not asked: does this touchpoint carry trust signal? Is this where customers feel seen? Is removing the human element consistent with the brand relationship we've built?

Those questions need to be in the room. The decision to automate a customer touchpoint is a brand decision. It should have brand leadership's fingerprints on it, with explicit assumptions about trust cost documented and revisited — not buried in an ops ticket.

"True direction means knowing what you add to people's lives, not just what you sell them." If the human interaction was part of what you added, removing it changes the equation. That change should be conscious, deliberate, and accounted for — not a side effect of a cost-optimization sprint.

---

## The Metric That's Missing

My actual recommendation is simple in principle, hard in practice: add a trust-cost assumption to every customer-facing automation proposal. Force the team to state, explicitly, what they believe this touchpoint is doing for brand relationship — and what their assumption is about the cost of removing the human element.

It doesn't have to be a precise number. It has to be a documented, auditable assumption that someone is accountable for revisiting when the NPS cohort data arrives 18 months later. Right now, there's no accountability loop because no one wrote down what they expected.

The organizations winning with AI right now aren't the ones automating fastest. They're the ones being deliberate about which touchpoints to protect — and treating that protection as a strategic choice, not a failure of efficiency.

Human presence, in the right interactions, is not an inefficiency to be optimized away. It's an asset that AI cannot replicate, and the market is already starting to price that in.

I watched a video recently that made a point I think many people still underestimate.

As AI makes it easier to build software, the build layer itself starts collapsing into a commodity. What looked like a moat a few years ago starts looking like table stakes. If anyone can generate an interface, wire together a workflow, launch a product, clone a feature set, or ship a polished MVP in a weekend, then the question changes.

It is no longer, can you build?

It becomes, why you?

That shift matters more than most strategy decks are willing to admit.

The video framed the next durable layers of value on the web as trust, context, distribution, liability, and taste. I think that is right. But I also think it points to something bigger.

This is not just a shift in software economics.

It is a visible symptom of what I’ve been calling the **Digital Singularity Shift**.

My core thesis has been simple: when technology drives functional excellence toward abundance, functional differentiation starts dying. Better, faster, cheaper, more automated, more personalized, more scalable, all of that keeps improving, but it stops being rare. And when function becomes abundant, value migrates upward and outward, toward the things that feel harder to replicate because they are more deeply human, more relational, more contextual, and more socially embedded.

That is exactly what this moment is showing us.

The web is not becoming less human because of AI.

In a strange way, it is becoming more brutally honest about where human value actually lives.

## The old internet rewarded production

For a long time, the internet rewarded the ability to make things.

Build the product. Ship the feature. Launch the website. Publish the content. Buy the traffic. Scale the funnel.

That world already had its gatekeepers, of course, but production still carried a kind of magic. If you could create at scale, you had leverage.

AI changes that equation.

Production is still useful, but it no longer guarantees advantage. It is being absorbed into infrastructure.

That is why so many categories suddenly feel unstable. The thing companies thought they were selling turns out to be the easiest part to reproduce.

Code becomes easier to generate.
Design becomes easier to generate.
Content becomes easier to generate.
Interfaces become easier to generate.
Even decisions start becoming easier to simulate.

So the economic center of gravity moves.

Not away from technology, but away from technology **as differentiation**.

## Trust becomes more valuable when the world gets noisier

When abundance explodes, certainty collapses.

That is why trust rises.

In a low-noise environment, people can compare options rationally and slowly. In a high-noise environment, they need shortcuts. Trust is one of the most powerful shortcuts we have.

And in the AI era, trust is not a soft brand word. It is hard infrastructure.

If an AI agent is helping me choose a product, a source, a service, a recommendation, a doctor, a school, a legal workflow, or a financial action, trust becomes part of the filtering logic. Not just for me as a human, but eventually for the systems acting on my behalf.

This matters because trust is earned socially and historically. It is built over time. It comes from consistency, clarity, behavior, transparency, and from what people say about you when you are not in the room.

An LLM can generate a landing page in seconds.
It cannot generate a reputation with the same depth.

That is one of the reasons I keep coming back to story, authenticity, and meaning. Not because they are abstract branding luxuries, but because in a commoditized environment they become decision architecture.

## Context is what generic intelligence lacks

AI is powerful, but generic intelligence is not the same thing as situated understanding.

A model can know a lot. That does not mean it knows *your* history, *your* constraints, *your* incentives, *your* people, or the subtleties of *this* exact moment.

That is context.

And context is becoming one of the most valuable assets on the web.

In practical terms, context is not just data. It is interpretation.
It is the backstory behind the brief.
It is why this customer behaves that way.
It is what happened last quarter.
It is what the team is afraid to say out loud.
It is what matters here, not in theory.

This is also why I believe the winners in the AI era will not simply be experts in the old sense. They will be orchestrators. People and organizations that know how to connect tools, signals, histories, incentives, and human realities into coherent action.

The more intelligence becomes abundant, the more valuable context becomes.

Because context is what turns raw capability into relevant judgment.

## Distribution still decides who matters

This part is deeply misunderstood by people who fall in love with the product layer.

The internet has always had a romantic bias toward builders. We love the idea that the best thing wins.

It rarely works that way.

Distribution decides what gets seen, what gets adopted, what becomes default, and what enters culture.

AI does not erase that. It amplifies it.

In fact, when more people can build more things faster, distribution becomes even more decisive because supply outgrows attention by an absurd margin.

That means audience, network, narrative position, community access, trust channels, repeat reach, and cultural relevance matter even more than before.

In my view, distribution is not separate from brand. It is one of the expressions of brand.

People do not distribute things just because they are functional.
They distribute things that make them feel something, say something, signal something, or belong to something.

That is why I think the future belongs to those who can create not just utility, but gravity.

## Liability is the moat most people don’t want to talk about

One of the most underrated forms of value in the AI era is simple: who is willing to stand behind the outcome?

That is liability.

As AI becomes capable of generating recommendations, actions, automations, decisions, drafts, diagnoses, and agents that act semi-autonomously, the question is not only what can be generated.

The question is who carries the consequences when it breaks.

Who signs off?
Who takes the legal risk?
Who absorbs the reputational damage?
Who guarantees a standard?
Who is accountable when the automation behaves badly in the real world?

This is where a lot of supposed disruption stories become less romantic.

Because the closer AI gets to real operational impact, the more institutions, customers, regulators, and markets start asking for responsibility, not just capability.

And responsibility is expensive.

That means liability itself becomes part of the product.

Not glamorous. Not viral. But incredibly durable.

In a world obsessed with generation, accountability becomes premium.

## Taste becomes strategic when content is infinite

If AI can produce endless options, then selection becomes more important than generation.

That is taste.

Taste is not decoration. It is judgment under abundance.

It is knowing what to leave out.
It is knowing what fits together.
It is sensing timing, tone, proportion, restraint, symbolism, energy, friction, and form.
It is making choices that feel obvious in hindsight and impossible to automate in advance.

This is true in media, product, hiring, writing, interface design, retail, culture, luxury, strategy, and leadership.

And yes, AI can mimic patterns of taste.
But mimicry is not the same as conviction.

Real taste has a point of view.
It comes from lived exposure, synthesis, standards, risk, and identity.
It is not just what looks good. It is what feels true, coherent, and worth backing.

In the Digital Singularity Shift, this matters enormously.

Because once function becomes cheap, aesthetic and narrative coherence start carrying more weight. The winners are not just the ones who can make more. They are the ones who can decide what matters.

## This is why meaning is moving closer to the center of business

When I say that the future belongs to story, some people hear that as a soft or poetic statement.

I don’t mean it that way.

I mean that as technology commoditizes production, the market starts rewarding things like belief, trust, identity, community, coherence, and direction. In other words, the layers of value that make humans choose, stay, advocate, forgive, and return.

AI does not kill brand. It reveals what brand actually was.

Not a logo.
Not an ad campaign.
Not a surface treatment.

Brand is accumulated trust.
Brand is contextual memory.
Brand is distribution power.
Brand is a willingness to carry liability.
Brand is taste made legible.

And when those things come together, something deeper emerges.

Meaning.

That is the real connection to the Digital Singularity thesis.

As functional value gets flattened, meaningful value gets more expensive, more strategic, and more defensible.

The future will not be won by companies that merely use AI.
It will be won by companies, creators, and institutions that know how to wrap intelligence in trust, steer it with context, move it through distribution, ground it in accountability, and shape it with taste.

That is the new stack.

## The real opportunity

I do not think this is bad news.

I think it is clarifying.

We are entering an era where being more human is not a branding posture. It is a strategic advantage.

Not human as in slow.
Human as in credible.
Human as in accountable.
Human as in culturally aware.
Human as in capable of conviction.
Human as in able to create meaning in a world drowning in generated output.

The build layer will keep getting cheaper.
That is not the tragedy.
That is the trigger.

Because once everyone can make almost anything, we finally get forced to answer the question that was always hiding underneath the surface:

Why should anyone care?

The strongest answers will not come from features alone.
They will come from trust, context, distribution, liability, taste, and the stories that hold them together.

That is not the end of the web.

I think it is the beginning of a more honest one.

The Five Eyes statement from this week has been covered mostly as a cybersecurity story. Intelligence agencies warning about AI-powered threats. Governments urging organizations to act. The usual frame.

I think that misses the most important sentence in the document.

---

Here's the line: *"A whole-of-organisation and whole-of-society response is required."*

Most people read that as IT-speak. Brief your CISO. Update the risk register. Buy a better endpoint tool. Move on.

That's not what it means.

---

Benjamin Simkin, in *The AI First Company*, makes an argument I keep coming back to. The reason most companies fail to benefit from AI isn't that they don't have the right tools. It's that they've never asked the right question. They ask: *how can AI improve what we already do?* That question anchors you to the existing architecture. Every answer is some version of bolt-on.

The right question, Simkin argues, is different: *if I were building this company today, from zero, knowing what AI can do, what would it look like?*

That question makes the architecture variable, not fixed. It opens up a redesign.

Whole-of-organisation is that question, applied at scale.

---

The Five Eyes agencies didn't publish a joint statement about firewall configurations. They published a statement about strategic posture. The framing was explicit: *"Cyber risk can no longer be treated as a purely technical issue. This is a core business risk and leadership responsibility."*

That's not addressed to your security team. It's addressed to your board.

And the implication runs deeper than most people are sitting with. When intelligence agencies from five countries say frontier AI will "fundamentally transform both offensive and defensive cyber capabilities" within months — not years — they're describing an environment where your current business architecture may not hold. Not because your firewall failed. Because the assumptions baked into how you built your company were designed for a different threat surface.

---

I tried to make sense of this in the context of what happened on June 12th.

The US government pulled Anthropic's Fable and Mythos models for foreign nationals with no warning. One evening, hundreds of millions of users globally lost access to AI tools they'd embedded into their workflows, their products, their operations. No appeal. No timeline.

The companies hit hardest weren't the ones who used those models the most. They were the ones who'd built around them without asking what happens when the access disappears. Their org structure, their delivery model, their client commitments — all of it assumed continuity of access. That assumption got revoked overnight.

That's not a security failure. That's a design failure.

---

Whole-of-organisation, done properly, means that the question of AI dependency isn't owned by IT. It's owned by everyone who makes structural decisions about how the company operates.

It means when your head of operations designs a client delivery workflow that routes through an external frontier model, she's making a geopolitical infrastructure decision, even if she doesn't know it. It means when your sales team builds a proposal process that runs through a vendor API, they're accepting a supply chain risk that belongs on the same level as any other critical dependency.

Simkin's framing is useful here: AI-first design isn't about where you put the tools. It's about who owns the architecture. Retrofitters put tools in the hands of individual teams and let the structure absorb them. AI-first builders make the structure itself the starting point, then decide where the tools go.

The Five Eyes statement is asking for the second approach. At a civilizational level.

---

My take on what "whole-of-organisation" actually requires in practice:

It means someone in your leadership team owns the question of AI architecture — not AI adoption. Adoption is what tools you use. Architecture is how those tools are positioned in relation to your core operations, and what happens when any one of them disappears.

It means your risk surface is defined by your design, not by your vendor's uptime SLA. External model access going dark isn't an IT incident. It's a business continuity event, and it should be treated with the same preparation as any other.

It means the clean-sheet question isn't a one-time exercise. It's a standing discipline. Every time the environment shifts — and the Five Eyes are telling you it's going to shift faster than most organizations are planning for — the question of what you'd build today is worth asking again.

---

The statement came from signals agencies that almost never speak publicly together. That coordination is itself a signal. They're not warning about a theoretical risk. They're describing something they can see in the data that the rest of us can't, and concluding that the private sector isn't moving at the right pace.

I don't think they're wrong about that.

The organizations that treat "whole-of-organisation" as an IT briefing item will still be updating their risk registers when the next Fable event happens. The ones that treat it as a design mandate — and actually restructure how they make decisions about AI dependency — will be in a different position.

Not because they were smarter. Because they asked the question at the right level.

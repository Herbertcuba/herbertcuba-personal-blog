IQ Got Automated. Wisdom Didn't.

## Subtitle (optional)
Two centuries of optimizing for the wrong kind of intelligence.

## Body (~420 words)

A frontier model just scored in the 99th percentile on the LSAT, cleared the USMLE, and passed competitive programming benchmarks that would have been the bragging rights of a prodigy a decade ago. The headlines framed it as intelligence catching up to humans.

I think we are measuring the wrong thing.

Every one of those benchmarks is a closed problem with a known answer. That is what IQ tests, and their professional cousins, have always measured — the ability to solve well-defined problems inside well-defined rules. For 150 years this was the dominant definition of *intelligence* in the West, and almost every institution we built — schools, credentials, hiring pipelines — was designed to select for it.

The machines have now caught up. The ceiling of human cognition turned out to be the easiest part to automate.

There have always been two kinds of intelligence. The first is **IQ** — raw analytical horsepower, symbol manipulation, logic chains, pattern recognition. The second is **wisdom** — the ability to decide which problems are worth solving, for whom, under what constraints, at what cost. Knowing when the question as asked is the wrong question. Tolerating uncertainty when the data runs out.

Aristotle made this distinction 2,400 years ago, separating *sophia* from *phronesis*. Modern psychology rediscovered it and measured it. Paul Baltes and Ursula Staudinger at the Max Planck Institute found that IQ and wisdom are largely uncorrelated. Igor Grossmann's longitudinal work at Waterloo shows wise reasoning predicts life satisfaction, relationship quality, and even mortality — better than IQ. Robert Sternberg, bluntly: *"We select future leaders for their ability to score well on tests of analytical intelligence, then we are surprised when they lead badly."*

Then the Flynn effect. IQ rose roughly three points per decade across the twentieth century. By almost any measure of wise judgment — long-term planning, polarization, institutional trust — we did not get wiser. We got smarter and not wiser, simultaneously, for a hundred years.

AI is the completion of that arc. It is IQ automation. It is not wisdom automation. It doesn't bear consequences, hold incommensurable values in tension, or recognize when the problem as specified is the wrong problem.

The bottleneck has shifted. For two centuries it was computation. It stopped being computation sometime in the last three years. The bottleneck now is judgment.

The old game was about being the smartest person in the room. That game is over, because the smartest entity in the room is no longer a person.

The new game is about being the wisest.

IQ got automated. Wisdom didn't.
